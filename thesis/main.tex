\section{Literature review}
\label{sec:lit-review}
% Review literature.
\citet{markowitz1952} established that an investor who cares only about the first two moments of portfolio returns should hold a portfolio on the efficient frontier. The mean-variance utility optimal (MVO) portfolio, which maximizes a scaled tradeoff risk to return portfolio utility subject to full investment and long-only constraints, requires an estimate of expected returns and an estimate of the covariance matrix, for which one can naturally choose sample covariance and sample returns as estimators, but it has been established when the number of assets large relative to data observations they are highly unreliable estimates. Even more interesting in \cite{campbellthompson2008} has definitively shown that any return estimate will be largely a noisy signal prediction as its explained variance has been proved to be theoretically bounded. In that line of though by the Marchenko-Pastur law, proved in \citet{marchenkopastur1967}, it has also been shown that the sample eigenvalues are systematically biased away from the true eigenvalues as large eigenvalues are inflated and small ones are deflated. \citet{michaud1989} termed this ``optimization error maximization'' with \citet{bestgrauer1991} establishing that optimal portfolios are extremely sensitive to small perturbations in the inputs. \citet{chopraziemba1993} quantified that result by finding that return errors are approximately ten times more harmful than covariance errors, yet both sources of error are large enough to cause economically significant performance degradation in practice. Later \citet{demiguel2009} conducted an empirical study concluding that across fourteen strategies and seven datasets, no sophisticated optimizer consistently outperformed the naive equal-weighted (EW) portfolio once estimation error was accounted for, and since this benchmark has been set a minimum hurdle for any new portfolio optimization strategy. 

However, numerous ailments for active management have been proposed. Some focus on optimizing covariance estimation, \citet{riskmetrics1996} introduced the exponentially weighted moving average (EWMA) covariance estimator, which assigns geometrically declining weights to past observations developed to respond more rapidly to volatility dynamics than an equal-weight rolling window. Furthermore, \citet{ledoitwolf2004} derived the analytically optimal linear shrinkage intensity toward a structured target, which also proved applicable to many fields outside of the realm of portfolio construction as well, and later in \citet{ledoitwolf2020} extended this to eigenvalue-by-eigenvalue nonlinear shrinkage (NLS) greatly improving estimation outcomes in practice at the cost of asymptotic consistency. 


There have also been many optimizations for return estimation techniques with early shrinkage developments like \cite{stein1956}, and most notably \citet{blacklitterman1992}, a Bayesian framework for blending equilibrium expected returns with investor views under prediction uncertainty, producing a posterior return signal (PRS) that is more stable than the sample mean and less sensitive to noise. Moreover, many relevant explanatory variables for return prediction have been found. For example, \citet{jegadeesh1993} documented that stocks with high returns in the recent past tend to continue to outperform, provided the most recent observations are skipped to avoid the short-run reversal effect. This momentum anomaly and many other inspired modern empirical asset pricing literature with first \cite{famafrench1993}, then \cite{carhart1997} and later \cite{famafrench2015}, which are currently among the most robust and reliable return estimation techniques.

In a parallel line of work, \citet{frostsavarino1988} first documented empirically that bounded weight constraints reduce portfolio variance with negligible loss in expected return, and \citet{jagannathanma2003} later formalized this insight by proving that imposing no-short-sale constraints on the minimum-variance portfolio is mathematically equivalent to applying a form of shrinkage to the covariance matrix. \citet{demiguelnogales2009} extended the result further by showing that constraints on the weight vector generalize this shrinkage interpretation and produce sparser, more stable portfolios that consistently outperform the unconstrained sample-based MVO portfolio.

Some recent approaches avoids matrix inversion entirely, relying on machine learning or algorithmic approaches for asset allocation. \citet{lopezdeprado2016} proposed Hierarchical Risk Parity (HRP), which focuses on building a similarity hierarchy from the asset correlation matrix and allocate risk recursively through the tree using variance-based bisection without ever inverting the estimated covariance matrix or including noisy return estimates. This pioneered a new modern approach to active management more robust to the inherent noise of the asset estimation.

However, there has not yet been proposed an approach to successfully alleviate the estimation errors of the mean-variance optimization out-of-sample by introducing portfolio building methods agnostic to noise. This is the proposition and contribution of this thesis.

Theoretically I introduce and motivate Hierarchical Mean Variance Allocation (HMVA), an approach that allocates weight to assets that optimize risk adjusted returns out-of-sample while in the presence of estimation error.

Empirically, I compare the proposed HMVA portfolio against with various benchmarks on monthly returns of the top 100 point-in-time S\&P500 Index constituents (SPY-100), and examining the overall performance of all approaches and their performance during crisis and calm markets, with and without transaction costs.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:meth}
\subsection{Notation}
% Describe the notation
I will denote the total observation count as $T$, and the total number of assets will be $N$, with their ratio then being noted as $P=N/T$. The returns will be a $T \times N$ return matrix $R$, where each row corresponds to the observation index $t$ and each column to the asset index $n$, noted as $R_{t,n}$ and log returns as $r_{t,n}$. When using a time-series window of the form $[t_{start}, t_{end}]$, we will denote that as $R_{t_{start}:t_{end}}$, where applied to a specific asset index $n$ the notation will be $R_{t_{start}:t_{end}, n}$. Furthermore, history length will be denoted as $T_h$ and rebalance interval length will be $T_r$. The true covariance will be denoted as $\Sigma$ and the true return signal will be $\mu$, and their estimators will always be denoted with hats as $\hat{\Sigma}$ and $\hat{\mu}$ and superscripts will be applied to them to clarify when a specific estimator is used. For example the sample estimates will be:

$$
\hat{\Sigma}^{Sample} = \frac{1}{T-1} \mathbf{R}^\top \left( \mathbf{I}_T - \frac{1}{T} \mathbf{1}_T \mathbf{1}_T^\top \right) \mathbf{R},
\quad \quad
\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}^{Sample}  = \frac{1}{T} \mathbf{R}^\top \mathbf{1}_T
$$
\subsection{Hierarchical Mean Variance Allocation}
Hierarchical Mean Variance Allocation (HMVA) constructs the binary tree top-down. Starting from the full asset universe, at each node the asset universe on the current cluster level, denoted $U$, is split into two disjoint subsets left and right denoted $L$ and $R$ by minimizing a blend of inter-cluster covariance and inter-cluster correlation computed from the equal-weighted sub-portfolios of each group:
\begin{equation}
  f(L, R) =
    \frac{1}{2}\underbrace{\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i \in L,\, j \in R}
                \hat{\Sigma}_{ij}}{n_L n_R}}_{\displaystyle\mathrm{Cov}_{\mathrm{EW}}(L,R)}
    + \frac{1}{2}\underbrace{\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i \in L,\, j \in R}
                \hat{\Sigma}_{ij}}{\sqrt{S_L S_R}}}_{\displaystyle \mathrm{Corr}_{\mathrm{EW}}(L,R)} 
  \label{eq:split-obj}
\end{equation}
where for a cluster denoted $C\in\{L,R\}$, $n_C = |C|$ is the cluster size and $S_C = \sum_{i,j \in C}\hat{\Sigma}_{ij} = n_C^2 \cdot \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}_{\mathrm{EW}}(C)$ is the sum of all entries in the within-cluster covariance block. The first term, $\mathrm{Cov}_{\mathrm{EW}}(L,R)$, is the covariance between the equal-weighted portfolios of $L$ and $R$. The second term, $\mathrm{Corr}_{\mathrm{EW}}(L,R)$, normalises by the product of the equal-weight sub-portfolio volatilities and equals the Pearson correlation between $\mathrm{EW}_L$ and $\mathrm{EW}_R$.

Minimising $f(L,R)$ encourages the two branches to be as independent as possible as small $\mathrm{Cov}_{\mathrm{EW}}$ means the branches co-move little in absolute terms, while small $\mathrm{Corr}_{\mathrm{EW}}$ means they co-move little relative to their own volatilities. The equal 50/50 weighting of the two terms balances absolute and relative inter-cluster dependence. Importantly, this criterion evaluates the co-movement of the entire sub-portfolios rather than that of the nearest individual asset pair, producing branches whose total risk is more predictable and whose diversification benefit is directly captured at the level that determines capital allocation.

Once the tree is constructed, capital is allocated top-down. At each internal node with left and right children $L$ and $R$, cluster Sharpe approximations for a cluster $C\in\{L,R\}$ will formed:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{S}(C) = \max\!\left(
    \frac{n_C^{-1}\sum_{i \in C}\hat{\mu}_{i}}
         {\sqrt{n_C^{-2}\sum_{i,j \in C}\hat{\Sigma}_{ij}}},\; 0
  \right),
  \label{eq:sharpe-proxy}
\end{equation}
where the numerator derived from the return estimation of $\hat{\mu}$ and the denominator is its equal-weight volatility derived from the estimation of $\hat{\Sigma}$. If both cluster Sharpe proxies are non-positive, to ensure weights remain non-negative, the allocation reverts to the inverse-variance bisection for the cluster $C\in\{L,R\}$:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{IV}(C) = 
    \frac{1}
         {\sqrt{n_C^{-2}\sum_{i,j \in C}\hat{\Sigma}_{ij}}}
  \label{eq:inv_var}
\end{equation}
The weight allocation to the left and right child is therefore:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_L = \frac{\hat{IV}(L)}{\hat{IV}(L)+\hat{IV}(R)} \quad \text{or} \quad
  \alpha_L = \frac{\hat{S}(L)}{\hat{S}(L)+\hat{S}(R)}, \qquad \alpha_R = 1-\alpha_L
  \label{eq:sharpe-alloc}
\end{equation}
so the branch with the higher risk-adjusted expected return receives more capital. The tree structure bounds the maximum allocation to any individual cluster, providing implicit concentration limits without hard constraints. To establish stability in the tree structure a allocation smoother is advised to reduce spurious allocations due to the fine-structure of the tree hierarchy.

\subsection{Estimation}
% Describe methods.
\subsubsection{Covariance Estimation}
Following the RiskMetrics convention \cite{riskmetrics1996}, returns are exponentially weighted with decay parameter $\theta = 0.5^{1/h}$ corresponding to a half-life of $T_\theta$ trading days. To inherit this weighting without modifying the shrinkage estimator that follows, the return matrix is rescaled into the pseudo-return matrix
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{R}_{t,\cdot} = \sqrt{w_t \, T}\; R_{t,\cdot}, \qquad
  w_t = \frac{(1-\theta)\,\theta^{\,T-1-t}}{\sum_{s=0}^{T-1}(1-\theta)\,\theta^{\,s}}, \qquad \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} w_t = 1,
  \label{eq:ewma-pseudo}
\end{equation}
so that the unweighted sample second moment of $\tilde{R}$ recovers the EWMA covariance:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\Sigma}^{\text{EWMA}} \;=\; T^{-1}\tilde{R}^{\top}\tilde{R} \;=\; \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} w_t\, R_{t,\cdot}^{\top} R_{t,\cdot}.
  \label{eq:ewma-cov}
\end{equation}
This rescaling allows any shrinkage estimator to be applied to $\tilde{R}$ as if it were a plain return matrix, automatically inheriting the exponential weighting without altering its internal calculations.

The eigendecomposition $\hat{\Sigma}^{\text{EWMA}} = U\Lambda U^{\top}$ with $\Lambda = \mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_N)$ is then corrected via the nonlinear shrinkage (NLS) estimator of \cite{ledoitwolf2012}, using the analytical implementation of \cite{ledoitwolf2020}. Each sample eigenvalue is mapped to its oracle counterpart according to
\begin{equation}
  \lambda_i^{\star} \;=\; \frac{\lambda_i}{\bigl|\,1 - c - c\, \lambda_i\, \tilde{m}_F(\lambda_i)\,\bigr|^{2}}, 
  \qquad c = \frac{N}{T},
  \label{eq:nls}
\end{equation}
where 
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{m}_F(\lambda) \;=\; \lim_{z \to \lambda + i 0^{+}} \int \frac{1}{t - z}\, dF(t)
  \label{eq:stieltjes}
\end{equation}
is the boundary value of the Stieltjes transform of the limiting spectral distribution $F$ of $\hat{\Sigma}^{\text{EWMA}}$, estimated nonparametrically via the kernel density formula of \cite{ledoitwolf2020}. The shrunk estimator is reconstructed from the original eigenvectors as
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\Sigma}^{\text{EWMA,NLS}} \;=\; U\,\mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1^{\star},\dots,\lambda_N^{\star})\,U^{\top},
  \label{eq:nls-cov}
\end{equation}
which delivers the asymptotically optimal correction under Frobenius loss within the class of rotation-equivariant estimators, leaving the eigenvectors of $\hat{\Sigma}^{\text{EWMA}}$ unchanged.
\subsubsection{Return Estimation}

Using the return matrix $R$ with a given history window with length $T_h$ and a covariance estimation $\hat{\Sigma}$ computed over the same return window, we calculate the momentum signal by skipping the most recent $T_s$ days and averaging returns over the interval $[T_h, T_s]$: 
\begin{equation}
    Q =  (T_h - T_s)^{-1}\sum_{T_h < t < T_s} R_{t}
    \label{eq:view_const}
\end{equation}
This is done to avoid the reversal effect in short-horizon momentum as proposed in \cite{jegadeesh1993}, and here we set $T_s=T_r$ to exclude momentum since the very last rebalance of the portfolio. Since we calculate the momentum signal for each assets, we construct a dense view $1 \times N$ vector $Q$, hence our view matrix will always be $P=I_N$, as there is one absolute view for every asset since no relative views are accounted for. The convention we adopt in this paper is that we set the view uncertainty to be the covariance of each asset, or explicitly as $\Omega = \text{diag}(\hat{\Sigma})$. The second convention introduced is that we will choose our prior to be an optimistic 1\% expected return for each asset to encourage participation in return maximization, or shown explicitly it is $1 \times N$ vector equal to $\pi=\mathbf{1}_n / 100$. Finally we can use the result of \cite{blacklitterman1992}:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mu}_{\text{PRS}} = \bigl[(\tau\hat{\Sigma})^{-1} + P^{\top}\Omega^{-1}P\bigr]^{-1}
                   \bigl[(\tau\hat{\Sigma})^{-1}\pi + P^{\top}\Omega^{-1}Q\bigr]
    \label{eq:BL_formula}
\end{equation}
and apply it, which simplifies when $P=I_N$ and $\Omega = \text{diag}(\hat{\Sigma})$ to the expression:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mu}_{\text{PRS}} = \pi + \hat{\Sigma} (\hat{\Sigma}+\text{diag}(\hat{\Sigma})^{-1}(Q-\pi)
    \label{eq:BL_simplified}
\end{equation}
where we evaluate the hyperparameter $\tau$ at $1$ for simplicity in the posterior formula, leaving us with a relatively noise-robust expression for momentum informed return signals.

\subsubsection{Allocation Smoothing}
To curb portfolio turnover generated by noise estimates of of $\hat{\mu}$ and $\hat{\Sigma}$ without fully discarding relevant new information, the target weights produced at each rebalance are blended with the previous period's weights through a Kalman-style update \cite{kalman1960}, in which the blend coefficients are adapted to a proxy for the prevailing signal-to-noise ratio of the estimation environment.

The covariance side of this ratio is captured by the normalized spectral entropy of $\hat{\Sigma}$ over the most recent return window $R_{t-T_r:t}$, a scalar measure of how concentrated or diffuse the estimated risk structure is \cite{meucci2009}:
\begin{equation}
  X_t \;=\; \frac{-\sum_{i=1}^{N} p_i \log p_i}{\log N}, \qquad
  p_i \;=\; \frac{\lambda_i}{\sum_{j=1}^{N} \lambda_j},
  \label{eq:kf-r}
\end{equation}
where $\{\lambda_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$ are the eigenvalues of $\hat{\Sigma}$ and the denominator $\log N$ rescales the raw entropy onto $[0,1]$. The lower bound is attained when a single eigenvalue dominates the spectrum, indicating a sharply identified principal direction of risk; the upper bound is attained when all eigenvalues are equal, in which case the covariance structure is maximally diffuse and individual factor loadings carry little information. A high $X_t$ therefore signals an ill-conditioned estimation environment in which the new target weights should be trusted less.

The return side is captured by the relative change in the posterior return estimate $\hat{\mu}$ between consecutive rebalances,
\begin{equation}
  Y_t \;=\; \frac{\|\hat{\mu}_{t} - \hat{\mu}_{t-T_r}\|_{}}{\|\hat{\mu}_{t-T_r}\|_{} + \epsilon},
  \label{eq:kf-q}
\end{equation}
where norm used is Euclidean and $\epsilon > 0$ is a small constant included for numerical stability against division by zero. A high $Y_t$ indicates that the expected-return vector has shifted substantially relative to the previous rebalance, which warrants a larger update to the portfolio.

Given the new target weights $w_t$ produced by the optimizer and the previously held weights $w_{t-T_r}$, the smoothed weights $\tilde{w}_t$ are formed as the signal-to-noise-weighted convex combination
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{w}_{t} \;=\; \frac{X_t\, w_{t-T_r} + Y_t\, w_t}{X_t + Y_t},
  \label{eq:kf-update}
\end{equation}
which can equivalently be written as the Kalman-style update
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{w}_t \;=\; (1 - K_t)\, w_{t-T_r} + K_t\, w_t, 
  \qquad K_t \;=\; \frac{Y_t}{X_t + Y_t} \;\in\; [0,1],
  \label{eq:kf-gain}
\end{equation}
with $K_t$ playing the role of a data-adaptive Kalman gain \cite{kalman1960}. When the covariance structure is well-identified and the return signal is approximately stationary, $K_t$ is close to zero and the portfolio barely moves, preserving transaction capacity; when the signal changes substantially relative to structural uncertainty, $K_t$ approaches one and the portfolio updates aggressively toward the new target. The smoothed weights $\tilde{w}_t$ are renormalized to sum to one after the update.

\subsection{Statistical Inference}
\label{inf_stats}

\subsubsection{Sharpe ratio test}
Following \cite{ledoitwolf2008}, the distribution of the Sharpe ratio difference $\widehat{\Delta\mathrm{SR}} = \widehat{\mathrm{SR}}^{1} - \widehat{\mathrm{SR}}^{2}$ is estimated by a circular block bootstrap, which preserves the serial dependence and higher-moment structure of the underlying return series. For two strategies with realised return series $\{R_t^{1}\}_{t=1}^{T}$ and $\{R_t^{2}\}_{t=1}^{T}$, the sample Sharpe ratios are
\[
\widehat{\mathrm{SR}}^{i} \;=\; \frac{\bar{R}^{\,i}}{\hat{\sigma}^{i}}, 
\qquad 
\bar{R}^{\,i} = T^{-1}\sum_{t=1}^{T} R_t^{i}, 
\qquad 
\big(\hat{\sigma}^{i}\big)^{2} = T^{-1}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\big(R_t^{i} - \bar{R}^{\,i}\big)^{2}.
\]
Paired return observations $(R_t^{1}, R_t^{2})$ are resampled jointly in overlapping blocks of length $T_r$ corresponding to one rebalance, with the series wrapped circularly so that every observation receives equal selection probability. For each of $B = 2000$ bootstrap replications, a pseudo-sample of length $T$ is drawn and the recentered difference
\[
d_b^{*} \;=\; \big(\widehat{\mathrm{SR}}^{1,*}_{b} - \widehat{\mathrm{SR}}^{2,*}_{b}\big) - \widehat{\Delta\mathrm{SR}}, 
\qquad b = 1,\dots,B,
\]
is computed, where the subtraction of $\widehat{\Delta\mathrm{SR}}$ centers the bootstrap distribution at zero to enforce the null $H_0:\mathrm{SR}^{1} = \mathrm{SR}^{2}$. The two-sided empirical $p$-value is
\[
\hat{p} \;=\; B^{-1}\sum_{b=1}^{B}\mathbf{1}\!\left[\,\big|d_b^{*}\big| \;\geq\; \big|\widehat{\Delta\mathrm{SR}}\big|\,\right],
\]
with the block length and replication count chosen in line with the recommendations of \cite{ledoitwolf2008} for monthly return data.

\subsubsection{Forecast comparison test}
Following \cite{dieboldmariano1995}, pairwise return series are compared via a test of equal predictive accuracy under a squared-loss criterion. For two competing strategies with realised return series $\{R_t^{1}\}_{t=1}^{T}$ and $\{R_t^{2}\}_{t=1}^{T}$, we define the loss differential as:

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{d}_t \;=\; \ell\!\left(R_t^{1}\right) - \ell\!\left(R_t^{2}\right), \qquad \ell(R_t^{i}) = \left(R_t^{i} - \bar{R}^{\,i}\right)^{2}
    \label{eq: dm_stat_start}
\end{equation}
so that $\tilde{d}_t$ measures the period $t$ gap in squared deviations between the two series. The test statistic is:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{DM} \;=\; \frac{\bar{\tilde{d}}}{\sqrt{\widehat{\mathrm{LRV}}(\tilde{d}_t)\,/\,T}}, 
\qquad 
\bar{\tilde{d}} = T^{-1}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \tilde{d}_t
\label{eq: dm_stat}
\end{equation}
where $\widehat{\mathrm{LRV}}(\tilde{d}_t)$ is the heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation consistent long-run variance of $d_t$, estimated with a \cite{neweywest1994} kernel using a bandwidth of $\lfloor T^{1/3} \rfloor$ lags to absorb the serial dependence induced by overlapping monthly observations. Under the null hypothesis $H_0:\mathbb{E}[\tilde{d}_t] = 0$ of equal predictive accuracy, $\mathrm{DM} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, and the two-sided $p$-value is $2\,[1-\Phi(|\mathrm{DM}|)]$. This complements the Sharpe bootstrap by evaluating the two strategies on a loss-function basis rather than a risk-adjusted-return basis.

\section{Data and Empirical Design}
\label{sec:data}
% Describe data.
\subsection{CRSP Data}
The empirical experiment uses daily CRSP data with four primary fields, which are \texttt{PERMNO} (unique stock identifier), \texttt{DlyCalDt} (calendar date), \texttt{DlyClose} (split-adjusted closing price), and \texttt{DlyRet} (daily total return). To populate the return matrix \texttt{DlyRet} is used directly when available for a given stock-date pair, otherwise the return is computed from consecutive adjusted closing prices. The rebalance window will be monthly, hence $T_r=21$ and the estimation history will be two years, thus $T_h=504$.

\subsection{Point-in-time universe}
\label{sec:data-pit}

S\&P~500 membership is reconstructed from a historical constituents file that records membership in the range format, such that each row identifies a PERMNO and the interval during which it was an S\&P500 constituent. This prevents survivorship bias as only stocks that were members of the index on the specific rebalance date are eligible for inclusion in the portfolio.

At each $T_r$ day rebalance date $t$, or otherwise stated every month, the investable universe is constructed by first finding the set of PERMNOs with active S\&P500 membership on $t$ from the constituents file, then restricting it to the top 100 by market capitalisation as of $t$, using the most recent available daily cap figure. Then we retain only those PERMNOs with complete return data profile over the full estimation history window $[t - T_{h}, t - 1]$. Stocks that fail this criterion are effectively excluded from all strategies at that rebalance, by setting their returns to for the period to $0$. 

The resulting universe changes at every rebalance date, reflecting actual index additions, deletions, and corporate events in real time. The backtest period spans January 2002 $1^{\text{st}}$ to December $31^{\text{st}}$ 2024, with the first usable rebalance date occurring after the two year warm-up period from the data start of January, $1^{\text{st}}$ 2000. Important to note is that for backtests including multiple different estimation history lengths, to ensure no variability due to new observations the largest estimation history length in days will be skipped and then estimation over the desired history will be conducted.
