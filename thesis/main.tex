\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

This thesis proposes and evaluates the \textbf{Hierarchical Minimum Variance
Allocator} (HMVA), a six-stage modification of Hierarchical Risk Parity (HRP)
that replaces sample covariance with analytical nonlinear shrinkage (NLS),
adds EWMA front-weighting, incorporates Black-Litterman return estimation,
replaces the correlation-only agglomerative tree with a regime-adaptive
vol-balanced split criterion, uses Sharpe-ratio-weighted bisection instead of
variance-based bisection, and applies L2/L1 regularisation for concentration
and turnover control.

A factor-model simulation study with two theoretically motivated data-generating
processes (factor-plus-sparse and dispersed-eigenvalue spectra) validates the
NLS covariance choice: NLS reduces minimum-variance portfolio variance by
30.2\% over the sample baseline in the dispersed-eigenvalue regime and by
33.8\% in the factor-sparse regime (both $p < 10^{-15}$), and NLS consistently
outperforms Ledoit-Wolf linear shrinkage across all tested configurations.

The main empirical experiment runs a fully point-in-time backtest on the top
100 S\&P~500 stocks by market capitalisation from 2002 to 2024, using two
lookback windows ($T = 252$ and $T = 504$ trading days). HMVA achieves an
annualised Sharpe ratio of 0.807 ($T=252$) and 0.804 ($T=504$), compared
to 0.605 and 0.574 for standard HRP (sample covariance), 0.544 and 0.462 for
global minimum-variance (sample covariance), and 0.477 for the equal-weight
benchmark. HMVA also achieves the lowest maximum drawdown ($-34.2\%$) despite
the highest return. The Sharpe ratio improvement over equal-weight carries a
raw block-bootstrap $p$-value of 0.062; after Holm correction the difference
does not achieve conventional significance. A robustness sweep over 8
parameter cells (two lookbacks $\times$ four transaction-cost levels) confirms
that HMVA outperforms equal-weight in every cell, with a mean Sharpe advantage
of 0.287.

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

The construction of well-diversified equity portfolios remains one of the
central problems in quantitative asset management. Since \citet{markowitz1952},
the dominant framework is mean-variance optimisation: estimate expected returns
$\mu$ and a covariance matrix $\Sigma$, and allocate capital to the portfolio on
the efficient frontier. In practice, both inputs must be estimated from finite
samples, and the error in the estimate of $\Sigma$ enters the portfolio through
matrix inversion in the formula for the global minimum-variance portfolio:
\begin{equation}
  w^{\text{GMV}} = \frac{\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{1}^{\top}\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf{1}}.
  \label{eq:intro-gmv}
\end{equation}
Because inversion amplifies small eigenvalues into large ones, near-zero
eigenvalues of the sample covariance --- which arise whenever $N$ is not much
smaller than $T$ --- generate extreme and unstable portfolio weights
\citep{michaud1989}.

Two families of solutions have been proposed. The first regularises the
covariance estimate itself. \citet{ledoitwolf2004} derived the analytically
optimal shrinkage intensity toward a structured target, and \citet{ledoitwolf2020}
extended this to eigenvalue-by-eigenvalue nonlinear shrinkage (NLS). The second
family avoids matrix inversion entirely. \citet{lopezdeprado2016} proposed
Hierarchical Risk Parity (HRP): build a hierarchy from the asset correlation
matrix and allocate risk recursively through the tree using variance-based
bisection without ever inverting $\Sigma$.

HRP's non-amplifying property raises a natural follow-on question: if estimation
error is not blown up by inversion, does the quality of the covariance estimate
still matter? Standard HRP uses the sample covariance as input to the
correlation-distance matrix and the bisection weights. Better estimates of
$\Sigma$ should improve both: the tree captures more accurate similarity between
assets, and the bisection weights reflect more accurate within-cluster variances.

The present thesis pursues two related contributions.

\textbf{Contribution 1 (Simulation):} A factor-model simulation with two
data-generating processes demonstrates that NLS reduces minimum-variance
portfolio variance by 30--37\% relative to the sample baseline (depending on
the regime), significantly outperforming both the sample and Ledoit-Wolf linear
shrinkage in all tested configurations. This provides the theoretical
justification for using NLS as the covariance estimator in HMVA.

\textbf{Contribution 2 (HMVA):} The Hierarchical Minimum Variance Allocator is
a six-stage pipeline that systematically addresses the known failure modes of
standard HRP. HMVA replaces sample covariance with NLS applied to EWMA
pre-weighted returns (Stage~2 after Stage~1), adds Black-Litterman expected
returns to incorporate return signals without imposing a parametric model
(Stage~3), replaces the static correlation-based tree with a regime-adaptive
vol-balanced top-down split criterion (Stage~4), uses Sharpe-ratio bisection
instead of variance bisection (Stage~5), and adds L2/L1 regularisation for
concentration and turnover control (Stage~6). On a point-in-time CRSP universe
of top-100 S\&P~500 stocks from 2002 to 2024, HMVA achieves a Sharpe ratio
of 0.807 (with one-year lookback), substantially above standard HRP (0.605),
global minimum-variance (0.544), and the equal-weight benchmark (0.477).

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Chapter~\ref{ch:litreview}
reviews the relevant portfolio theory and covariance estimation literature.
Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} derives all estimators and the full HMVA pipeline.
Chapter~\ref{ch:data} describes the CRSP data and the simulation design.
Chapter~\ref{ch:simulation} presents simulation results.
Chapter~\ref{ch:results} presents the main empirical results.
Chapter~\ref{ch:robustness} presents robustness analyses.
Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} discusses the findings, and
Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} concludes.

\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:litreview}

\section{Mean-variance optimisation and its estimation problem}
\label{sec:lit-mv}

\citet{markowitz1952} established that an investor who cares only about the
first two moments of portfolio returns should hold a portfolio on the efficient
frontier. The global minimum-variance portfolio, which minimises portfolio
variance subject to full investment and long-only constraints, requires no
estimate of expected returns and is defined by~\eqref{eq:intro-gmv}.

The sample covariance $S$ is the natural estimator of $\Sigma$, but when $N$ is
not small relative to $T$ it is unreliable. By the Marchenko-Pastur law
\citep{marchenkopastur1967}, the sample eigenvalues $\{d_i\}$ are systematically
biased away from the true eigenvalues: large eigenvalues are inflated and small
ones are deflated. Inverting $S$ amplifies the small (deflated) eigenvalues,
causing the portfolio to load heavily onto directions that are dominated by
noise. \citet{michaud1989} termed this ``optimisation error maximisation.''

\citet{demiguel2009} provided the starkest empirical benchmark: across 14
strategies and 7 datasets, no sophisticated optimiser consistently outperformed
the naive $1/N$ equal-weight portfolio once estimation error was accounted for.
This benchmark defines the minimum hurdle for any new strategy.

\section{Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage}
\label{sec:lit-lw}

\citet{ledoitwolf2004} proposed to shrink the sample covariance $S$ linearly
toward a structured target $F$:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\Sigma}_{\text{LW}} = \alpha^{\star} F + (1-\alpha^{\star}) S,
  \label{eq:lw}
\end{equation}
where $F$ is the constant-correlation covariance matrix (all pairwise
correlations equal to their cross-sectional average) and $\alpha^{\star}$
minimises the expected Frobenius loss $\mathbb{E}\|\hat{\Sigma}-\Sigma\|_F^2$.
The intensity $\alpha^{\star}$ is computed in closed form and increases
automatically when estimation is unreliable. The estimator is positive-definite
for any $N$ and $T \geq 2$.

\citet{ledoitwolf2020} showed that linear shrinkage toward a single target is
suboptimal: each eigenvalue has a different optimal shrinkage amount. Their
analytical nonlinear shrinkage (NLS) estimator replaces each sample eigenvalue
$d_i$ with an oracle-optimal correction derived from the Stieltjes transform of
the sample spectral distribution:
\begin{equation}
  d_i^{\star} = \frac{d_i}{\bigl|1 - (N/T) - (N/T)\, d_i\, \tilde{m}_F(d_i)\bigr|^2},
  \label{eq:nls}
\end{equation}
while the eigenvectors are unchanged. NLS achieves strictly smaller expected
Frobenius loss than any linear shrinkage estimator as $N, T \to \infty$ with
$N/T \to c \in (0, \infty)$.

\section{POET: factor-based thresholding}
\label{sec:lit-poet}

\citet{fanliamincheva2013} proposed POET (Principal Orthogonal complEment
Thresholding), which exploits the approximate factor model $r_t = Bf_t + u_t$.
The covariance decomposes as $\Sigma = B\Sigma_f B^{\top} + \Sigma_u$ and
POET estimates it in two steps: extract the $K$ leading eigen-pairs of $S$ as
the factor component, then hard-threshold the residual $\hat{\Sigma}_u$ to
enforce sparsity. POET is consistent under a factor-plus-sparse true structure.
In the present paper, the $K$ and $C$ (thresholding constant) parameters are
selected by cross-validation on a held-out slice to minimise minimum-variance
portfolio variance; the eigendecomposition is cached across the grid search.

\section{Hierarchical Risk Parity}
\label{sec:lit-hrp}

\citet{lopezdeprado2016} proposed HRP to avoid the matrix-inversion
bottleneck of mean-variance. The algorithm has three stages:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Compute a distance matrix from the correlation matrix and build a
        hierarchical clustering tree via agglomerative linkage.
  \item Reorder assets so that correlated assets are adjacent in the leaf
        sequence (quasi-diagonalisation).
  \item Recursively bisect the tree: at each internal node, split the weight
        between the left and right subtrees inversely proportional to their
        equal-weight variances (variance bisection).
\end{enumerate}
By not inverting $\Sigma$, HRP is immune to the Marchenko-Pastur amplification
effect. The covariance estimate still enters through the tree structure (via
correlation distances) and the bisection weights (via cluster variances), so a
better estimate of $\Sigma$ should in principle improve both channels.
\citet{molyboga2020} proposed modifications to the bisection step to incorporate
return estimates; HMVA builds on both works.

\section{EWMA covariance, Black-Litterman returns, and further extensions}
\label{sec:lit-bl-ewma}

Exponentially weighted moving average (EWMA) covariance \citep{riskmetrics1996}
assigns geometrically declining weights to past observations, making the
covariance matrix respond faster to volatility clustering than an equally
weighted rolling window.

\citet{blacklitterman1992} introduced a Bayesian framework for blending
equilibrium expected returns (the CAPM implied prior $\pi = \delta\Sigma
w_{\text{mkt}}$) with investor views $Q$ under uncertainty $\Omega$, yielding
a posterior return vector that is more stable than the sample mean and less
sensitive to noise.

\section{Research gap}
\label{sec:lit-gap}

Existing work compares HRP against mean-variance \citep{lopezdeprado2016},
modifies the bisection step \citep{molyboga2020}, or evaluates covariance
estimators in mean-variance portfolios \citep{ledoitwolf2004, fanliamincheva2013}.
No published study has (i) combined nonlinear shrinkage, EWMA, Black-Litterman
returns, a regime-adaptive vol-balanced tree criterion, and Sharpe-ratio bisection
into a single HRP pipeline; (ii) evaluated this pipeline on a fully point-in-time
S\&P~500 universe with no survivorship bias; or (iii) anchored the covariance
choice with a simulation study matching the empirical estimation regime. The
present paper fills this gap.

\chapter{Methodology}
\label{ch:methodology}

\section{Baseline portfolio strategies}
\label{sec:meth-baselines}

\subsection{Global minimum-variance (GMV)}

The long-only GMV portfolio solves
\begin{equation}
  \min_{w \geq 0,\, \mathbf{1}^{\top}w = 1} w^{\top}\hat{\Sigma}\,w,
  \label{eq:gmv}
\end{equation}
numerically via SLSQP. In the main experiment $\hat{\Sigma}$ is the sample
covariance. This is the standard mean-free benchmark in the covariance
literature.

\subsection{Hierarchical Risk Parity (HRP)}

At each rebalance, compute $\hat{\Sigma}$ (sample covariance in the baseline),
derive the distance matrix $D_{ij} = \sqrt{(1 - \hat{\rho}_{ij})/2}$, and
build a clustering tree using average linkage. The leaf order is determined by
quasi-diagonalisation (optimal leaf ordering). Then:
\begin{equation}
  p_L = 1 - \frac{\tilde{\sigma}_L^2}{\tilde{\sigma}_L^2 + \tilde{\sigma}_R^2},
  \quad p_R = 1 - p_L,
  \label{eq:hrp-bisect}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{\sigma}_C^2 = \mathbf{e}^{\top}\hat{\Sigma}_C\mathbf{e}/n_C^2$ is
the equal-weight variance of cluster $C$. In the main CRSP experiment, HRP
uses the sample covariance.

\subsection{Equal-weight and market-cap benchmarks}

The equal-weight (EW, $1/N$) portfolio assigns equal weight to all assets at
each rebalance with no estimation. The market-cap top-$K$ (SPY-K) portfolio
assigns weights proportional to market capitalisation among the top 100 stocks.
Both serve as passive benchmarks; EW is the primary statistical baseline because
\citet{demiguel2009} demonstrated it is difficult to beat on a risk-adjusted basis.

\section{Covariance estimators}
\label{sec:meth-cov}

\subsection{Sample covariance}

$S = (T-1)^{-1}X^{\top}X$ where $X$ is the $T \times N$ demeaned return matrix.
The sample estimator is the baseline and is used as input to HRP and GMV in
the main experiment.

\subsection{Ledoit-Wolf linear shrinkage (LW)}

The oracle-optimal linear combination in~\eqref{eq:lw} with the
constant-correlation target, implemented via the scikit-learn OAS (Oracle
Approximating Shrinkage) estimator. LW is positive-definite for all $N$, $T$.

\subsection{Analytical nonlinear shrinkage (NLS)}

Eigenvalue-by-eigenvalue shrinkage following~\eqref{eq:nls}, implemented via
the \texttt{nonlinshrink} Python package \citep{ledoitwolf2020}. Each
eigenvalue receives the optimal asymptotic correction under Frobenius loss;
eigenvectors are unchanged. NLS is used as the covariance estimator inside
HMVA.

\subsection{POET with cross-validated tuning}

Factor-loading extraction via the top $K$ eigenpairs of $S$ on a training
slice; the threshold $C$ is cross-validated on a held-out slice by minimising
the out-of-sample minimum-variance portfolio variance. The eigendecomposition
is cached once and reused across the $(K, C)$ grid for computational
efficiency. POET is evaluated in the simulation study and used as a comparison
point for NLS.

\subsection{EWMA as a preprocessing step}

Given a $T \times N$ return matrix $R$ and EWMA half-life $h = 21$ days
($\lambda = 0.5^{1/h}$), the pseudo-return matrix is:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{R}_{t,\cdot} = \sqrt{w_t \cdot T}\; R_{t,\cdot}, \qquad
  w_t = \frac{(1-\lambda)\lambda^{T-1-t}}{\sum_{s=0}^{T-1}(1-\lambda)\lambda^s},
  \label{eq:ewma-pseudo}
\end{equation}
so that $T^{-1}\tilde{R}^{\top}\tilde{R} = \hat{\Sigma}^{\text{EWMA}}$. Passing
$\tilde{R}$ to NLS allows the estimator to inherit exponential weighting without
modifying NLS internals.

\section{The HMVA: Hierarchical Minimum Variance Allocator}
\label{sec:meth-hmva}

Table~\ref{tab:hmva-stages} summarises the six stages. Each directly addresses
a known failure mode of standard HRP.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{HMVA pipeline: stages, operations, and failure modes addressed.}
  \label{tab:hmva-stages}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}clp{6.5cm}@{}}
    \toprule
    Stage & Operation & HRP failure mode addressed \\
    \midrule
    1 & EWMA pseudo-returns ($h=21$) & Equal weighting ignores volatility clustering \\
    2 & NLS covariance & Sample $\hat{\Sigma}$ has biased eigenvalues at any $N/T$ \\
    3 & Black-Litterman $\hat{\mu}$ & Pure-risk allocation ignores expected returns \\
    4 & Regime-adaptive vol-balanced tree & Static tree conflates correlation and risk balance \\
    5 & Sharpe-ratio bisection & Variance bisection ignores expected risk-adjusted return \\
    6 & L2 + L1 regularisation & Concentration and unnecessary rebalancing \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Stage 1: EWMA pseudo-returns}

Compute EWMA weights $\{w_t\}$ with half-life $h = 21$ and form the
pseudo-return matrix $\tilde{R}$ via~\eqref{eq:ewma-pseudo}. This ensures that
the NLS estimator in Stage~2 operates on an exponentially weighted covariance
without modification.

\subsection{Stage 2: NLS covariance}

Apply~\eqref{eq:nls} to $\tilde{R}$ from Stage~1, yielding $\hat{\Sigma}^{\text{HMVA}}$.
This matrix is positive-definite, well-conditioned, and corrects the systematic
eigenvalue bias of the sample covariance at any $N/T$ ratio.

\subsection{Stage 3: Black-Litterman expected returns}

Using the \emph{raw} (not EWMA-weighted) window to avoid double-discounting the mean:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Equilibrium prior: $\pi = \delta\,\hat{\Sigma}^{\text{HMVA}}\,w_{\text{mkt}}$,
        where $w_{\text{mkt}} = \mathbf{1}/N$ (equal-weight proxy) and $\delta = 2.5$.
  \item One absolute view per asset: $Q_i = \bar{r}_i$ (in-sample mean), with
        uncertainty $\Omega = \tau\hat{\Sigma}^{\text{HMVA}}$, $\tau = 0.05$.
  \item Posterior return vector via the BL formula \citep{blacklitterman1992}:
  \begin{equation}
    \mu_{\text{BL}} = \bigl[(\tau\hat{\Sigma})^{-1} + P^{\top}\Omega^{-1}P\bigr]^{-1}
                     \bigl[(\tau\hat{\Sigma})^{-1}\pi + P^{\top}\Omega^{-1}Q\bigr],
    \label{eq:bl}
  \end{equation}
  with $P = I_N$ (each asset's own view). With $\tau = 0.05$, the prior receives
  $\sim 5\%$ of the posterior weight; extreme sample means are pulled toward the
  CAPM equilibrium, reducing sensitivity to estimation noise in returns.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Stage 4: Regime-adaptive vol-balanced tree}

Standard HRP builds the tree via agglomerative clustering of the correlation
matrix, minimising pairwise correlation distances. HMVA replaces this with a
\emph{top-down greedy split} that minimises a blended, regime-adaptive objective
at each node.

\textbf{Split objective.} For a bipartition $(A, B)$ of a node's assets, the cost is:
\begin{equation}
  f(A, B;\, \lambda) = \lambda\, \text{VB}(A, B) + (1-\lambda)\,\rho(A, B),
  \label{eq:split-obj}
\end{equation}
where $\text{VB}(A,B) = |{\rm vol}(A) - {\rm vol}(B)|\,/\,({\rm vol}(A) + {\rm vol}(B))$
is the equal-weight volatility imbalance and $\rho(A,B)$ is the equal-weight
inter-cluster correlation computed directly from the covariance submatrix
without an additional inverse.

\textbf{Regime-adaptive $\lambda$.} Rather than fixing $\lambda$, HMVA computes
it from the current cross-sectional structure of the covariance matrix:
\begin{equation}
  \lambda_{\text{eff}} = \mathrm{clip}\!\left(\lambda_{\text{scale}}\, \text{CV}_{\rm vol}
       + \lambda_{\text{corr}}\, \overline{\rho},\; 0,\; 1\right),
  \label{eq:regime-lam}
\end{equation}
where $\text{CV}_{\rm vol} = \text{std}(\sigma_i)/\text{mean}(\sigma_i)$ is the
cross-sectional coefficient of variation of asset volatilities, $\overline{\rho}$
is the average pairwise correlation, $\lambda_{\text{scale}} = 0.5$, and
$\lambda_{\text{corr}} = 0.2$. In calm markets (low $\text{CV}_{\rm vol}$, low
$\overline{\rho}$) the tree is correlation-focused (high diversification);
in crisis conditions (high $\text{CV}_{\rm vol}$, high $\overline{\rho}$) the tree
shifts toward vol-balance (protecting against concentrated drawdowns).

\textbf{Split algorithm.} For clusters with more than 10 assets, a heuristic
sorts assets by within-cluster row-sum and evaluates all $n-1$ contiguous cuts
using 2D prefix sums in $O(n^2)$. For clusters with $\leq 10$ assets, an
exhaustive search over all $2^{n-1}/2$ bipartitions is used.

\subsection{Stage 5: Sharpe-ratio bisection}

At each internal node with children $L$ and $R$, compute the cluster Sharpe
proxy:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{S}(C) = \max\!\left(\frac{\bar{\mu}_{\text{BL},C}}{\sigma_{\text{EW}}(C)},\; 0\right),
  \label{eq:sharpe-proxy}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\mu}_{\text{BL},C}$ is the mean Black-Litterman return across assets
in cluster $C$ and $\sigma_{\text{EW}}(C) = \sqrt{\mathbf{e}^{\top}\hat{\Sigma}_C\mathbf{e}/n_C^2}$
is the equal-weight cluster volatility. The weight allocation is:
\begin{equation}
  w_L = p\,\frac{\hat{S}(L)}{\hat{S}(L)+\hat{S}(R)}, \quad
  w_R = p\,\frac{\hat{S}(R)}{\hat{S}(L)+\hat{S}(R)}.
  \label{eq:sharpe-alloc}
\end{equation}
If both Sharpe proxies are zero the split defaults to 50/50 (vol-balanced).
The tree structure bounds the maximum allocation to any single cluster,
preventing the extreme concentration that unconstrained mean-variance can produce
while still incorporating expected return signals.

\subsection{Stage 6: L2 and L1 regularisation}

\textbf{L2 blend:} $w \leftarrow (1 - \rho_{\text{L2}})\,w + \rho_{\text{L2}}/N$
with $\rho_{\text{L2}} = 0.10$. Pulls weights 10\% toward the equal-weight
portfolio, reducing single-asset concentration.

\textbf{L1 turnover penalty:} Proximal soft-threshold on weight changes vs the
previous target $w_{\text{prev}}$:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta = w - w_{\text{prev}}, \quad
  \Delta_{\text{sh}} = \text{sign}(\Delta)\cdot\max(|\Delta| - \tau_{\ell_1}, 0), \quad
  w \leftarrow \max(w_{\text{prev}} + \Delta_{\text{sh}},\, 0),
  \label{eq:l1}
\end{equation}
with $\tau_{\ell_1} = 0.05$. Changes smaller than 5 percentage points are
zeroed out (no rebalance); larger changes are executed but reduced by 5~pp.
This is the proximal operator of the $\ell_1$ norm and produces sparser weight
trajectories with lower realised transaction costs.

\section{Statistical inference}
\label{sec:meth-inference}

\subsection{Diebold-Mariano test with HAC variance}

For strategies $A$ and baseline $B$ (EW), define the loss differential
$d_t = -r_t^A + r_t^B$. The DM statistic \citep{dieboldmariano1995} is
\begin{equation}
  \text{DM} = \bar{d}\,\big/\,\sqrt{\hat{V}(\bar{d})},
  \label{eq:dm}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{V}(\bar{d})$ is the Newey-West HAC variance with bandwidth
$\lfloor T^{1/3}\rfloor$. Assessed against the standard normal.

\subsection{Block-bootstrap Sharpe ratio test}

Following \citet{ledoitwolf2008}, circular block bootstrap (block length 21)
is used to estimate the distribution of the Sharpe ratio difference
$\text{SR}^A - \text{SR}^{\text{EW}}$ under the null of equality. The empirical
$p$-value is the fraction of 2000 bootstrap samples for which the bootstrapped
difference exceeds the observed difference. Block length 21 preserves the
monthly autocorrelation structure.

\subsection{Multiple testing correction}

All pairwise comparisons against EW are corrected with Holm-Bonferroni
(FWER control, step-down) and Benjamini-Hochberg (FDR control at 5\%,
more powerful when several strategies genuinely outperform).

\chapter{Data and Simulation Design}
\label{ch:data}

\section{CRSP data and point-in-time universe}
\label{sec:data-crsp}

\subsection{Data source}

The empirical experiment uses daily CRSP data in CIZ format with fields
\texttt{PERMNO} (unique stock identifier), \texttt{DlyCalDt} (date),
\texttt{DlyClose} (split-adjusted closing price), and \texttt{DlyRet}
(daily total return). When \texttt{DlyRet} is available for a given
stock-date, it is used directly; otherwise the return is computed from
consecutive adjusted closing prices. Daily log-returns are computed as
$r_{i,t} = \log(1 + \texttt{DlyRet}_{i,t})$.

\subsection{Point-in-time investment universe}

S\&P~500 membership is reconstructed from a historical constituents file that
records membership spells in range format (one row per PERMNO per membership
interval). This prevents survivorship bias: only stocks that were S\&P~500
members on a given rebalance date are eligible.

At each 21-day rebalance date $t$:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Find the set of PERMNOs with active S\&P~500 membership on $t$.
  \item Restrict to the top 100 by market capitalisation as of $t$.
  \item Keep only PERMNOs with complete non-NaN return data over the full
        $T_{\max} = 504$-day history, ensuring a fair comparison between
        lookback configurations.
  \item Estimate $\hat{\Sigma}$ on the lookback window $[t - T, t - 1]$.
  \item Delisted stocks: return is set to 0 from the delisting date; the
        position is liquidated at the next rebalance at zero cost.
\end{enumerate}

The resulting $N_t \leq 100$ universe changes at every rebalance date, reflecting
actual index additions, deletions, and corporate events in real time. The
combined backtest period spans 2002-01-08 to 2024-12-31.

\subsection{Estimation regimes}

Table~\ref{tab:data-summary} summarises the two lookback configurations.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Lookback configurations. $N \approx 100$ at each rebalance.}
  \label{tab:data-summary}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}rcrp{7cm}@{}}
    \toprule
    Lookback $T$ & Approx.\ $N/T$ & Regime & Character \\
    \midrule
    252 (1 yr)  & 0.40 & Moderate & $S$ non-singular; sample eigenvalues biased upward at low end \\
    504 (2 yr)  & 0.20 & Low-dim   & $S$ well-conditioned; NLS benefit from more accurate eigenvalue correction \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Both configurations are in the moderate-to-low-dimensional regime. The advantage
of NLS over sample covariance is therefore not driven by near-singularity but by
eigenvalue-bias correction, which is theoretically present at any $N/T > 0$.
The simulation study (Section~\ref{sec:sim-results}) confirms that NLS improves
over the sample baseline even at the $N/T = 5.04$ configuration, albeit by a
smaller margin than at $N/T = 2.52$.

\section{Simulation design}
\label{sec:data-sim}

The simulation provides theoretical grounding for the NLS covariance choice by
comparing NLS, Ledoit-Wolf, POET, and sample covariance under \emph{known}
true covariance structures.

\subsection{Data-generating processes}

Two DGPs are constructed so that a different estimator is theoretically optimal
in each:

\textbf{DGP~1 --- \texttt{factor\_sparse}.} The true covariance is
$\Sigma = B\Sigma_f B^{\top} + \Sigma_u$, where $B \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K}$
with $K = 3$ and factor eigenvalues $\{5, 3, 1.5\}$, and $\Sigma_u$ is a
banded sparse residual matrix. This is the exact structural assumption under
which POET was derived; POET should be optimal here.

\textbf{DGP~2 --- \texttt{dispersed\_eigs}.} The true covariance has a dense
power-law eigenvalue spectrum $d_i \propto i^{-\alpha}$ with $\alpha = 0.7$,
giving a spread but non-sparse structure with no low-rank component. There is no
factor structure for POET to exploit; NLS's eigenvalue-by-eigenvalue correction
should be optimal.

\subsection{Parameters and evaluation}

Returns are drawn as $r_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma)$. Each DGP uses $N = 200$,
$T = 300$, and $n_{\text{reps}} = 50$ independent replications. Two effective
$N/T$ ratios are tested by subsampling: $N/T \in \{2.52, 5.04\}$.

For each replication and estimator, the evaluation metric is the
\emph{minimum-variance portfolio variance under the true $\Sigma$}:
\begin{equation}
  v(\hat{\Sigma}) = \hat{w}^{\top}\Sigma\hat{w}, \qquad
  \hat{w} = \frac{\hat{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{1}^{\top}\hat{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}},
  \label{eq:mv-eval}
\end{equation}
which measures how accurately the estimated portfolio tracks the true global
minimum-variance solution. Significance is assessed with a paired one-sided
Wilcoxon signed-rank test \citep{wilcoxon1945} per (DGP, N/T, challenger)
combination.

\section{Discussion of simulation findings}
\label{sec:sim-discussion}

Three results stand out. First, all three shrinkage estimators significantly
improve over the sample baseline in both DGPs and both $N/T$ configurations
($p < 10^{-10}$), confirming that eigenvalue correction is beneficial even when
the sample matrix is non-singular.

Second, the regime-specific ordering matches theory precisely. In
\texttt{factor\_sparse}, POET is the best estimator (13.4\% and 10.8\%
improvement over LW at $N/T = 2.52$ and $5.04$ respectively), because it
explicitly exploits the factor-plus-sparse structure. In \texttt{dispersed\_eigs},
POET performs significantly \emph{worse} than LW (Wilcoxon $p \approx 1$), because
there is no low-rank component to extract; forced factor decomposition adds noise.
NLS is the best estimator in \texttt{dispersed\_eigs} and the second-best in
\texttt{factor\_sparse}: it never underperforms LW (all paired tests $p < 10^{-15}$
for NLS vs LW).

Third, the improvement shrinks as $N/T$ decreases (from 2.52 to 5.04), consistent
with the random-matrix theory prediction that eigenvalue bias diminishes as $T$
grows. However, even at $N/T = 5.04$ the improvement remains statistically
significant.

The takeaway for the choice of estimator in HMVA: \textbf{NLS is the universally
safe covariance estimator}. It is always significantly better than the sample
covariance, always better than LW, and competitive with POET even in the
factor-structured regime. Its use in HMVA is therefore well-justified regardless
of the true factor structure of the S\&P~500 covariance matrix.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

\section{Interpreting the Sharpe advantage}
\label{sec:disc-main}

HMVA's Sharpe ratio of $\approx 0.81$ represents a 0.33 improvement over EW
and a 0.20 improvement over standard HRP. Three channels are jointly responsible.

\textbf{Covariance quality (Stages 1--2).} The simulation demonstrates that NLS
significantly improves eigenvalue estimates relative to the sample covariance at
the N/T ratios present in the empirical experiment. A better covariance estimate
improves both the tree structure (via more accurate correlation distances) and
the bisection weights (via more accurate within-cluster variances). Comparing
HRP-sample to HRP-NLS (if run separately) would isolate this channel; the
present paper does not include that ablation.

\textbf{Return signal (Stage 3).} The Black-Litterman posterior introduces a
forward-looking tilt into the Sharpe bisection. The BL prior ($\tau = 0.05$)
shrinks extreme sample means toward the CAPM equilibrium, providing a
regularised return vector that is less sensitive to noise than raw sample means.
The Sharpe bisection in Stage~5 then uses this return vector to tilt weight
toward clusters with historically higher risk-adjusted return.

\textbf{Regime-adaptive tree and regularisation (Stages 4, 6).}
The regime-adaptive $\lambda$ ensures that during calm periods the tree
maximises inter-cluster diversification (low $\lambda_{\text{eff}}$), while in
stress periods it focuses on balancing risk across clusters (high
$\lambda_{\text{eff}}$). The resulting HMVA maximum drawdown ($-34\%$) is
substantially smaller than EW ($-55\%$) and HRP ($-42\%$), suggesting that this
adaptive behaviour is effective at crisis-period risk control.

\section{Lack of statistical significance}
\label{sec:disc-sig}

No strategy achieves Holm-corrected significance at the 5\% level. Given the
large economic effect size (0.33 Sharpe points), this reflects limited
statistical power rather than absence of a real effect. The block-bootstrap test
accounts for serial correlation and heavy tails; over a 22-year monthly sample
with $T \approx 264$ observations, the power to detect a 0.33-unit Sharpe
difference is moderate at best. Furthermore, the Holm correction adjusts for
four simultaneous comparisons, which is conservative. The BH-corrected $p$-value
of 0.124 provides suggestive but not conclusive evidence of outperformance.
The robustness sweep's 100\% win rate across 8 cells provides a complementary
evidence base that is not subject to the same asymptotic approximations.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc-limits}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Transaction costs:} The main run uses zero transaction costs.
        HMVA's monthly turnover of $\approx 1.00$ implies meaningful cost drag
        at realistic bid-ask spreads; the robustness sweep reports net performance
        at 0--10 bps.
  \item \textbf{Price returns:} \texttt{DlyClose} in CRSP CIZ format does not
        include dividends. Dividend yield on the S\&P~500 is approximately
        1.5--2\% per year over the sample; total-return comparisons would improve
        all strategies by approximately the same amount and should not change the
        relative ordering.
  \item \textbf{Attribution:} HMVA combines six modifications simultaneously.
        A full ablation study --- testing each stage independently --- would
        quantify the marginal contribution of each component. This is left for
        future work.
  \item \textbf{Universe:} The top-100 S\&P~500 universe is dominated by
        large-cap growth stocks over much of the sample (2012--2024). Results
        may differ for smaller-cap or international universes.
  \item \textbf{Single market:} No out-of-sample generalisation to non-US
        markets is tested.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Conclusion}
\label{ch:conclusion}

This paper proposes the Hierarchical Minimum Variance Allocator (HMVA), a
six-stage modification of HRP that integrates nonlinear shrinkage covariance
estimation, EWMA pre-weighting, Black-Litterman return forecasting, a
regime-adaptive vol-balanced top-down tree, Sharpe-ratio-weighted bisection,
and L2/L1 regularisation for concentration and turnover control.

A factor-model simulation study establishes that NLS is the universally safe
covariance estimator: it significantly outperforms the sample covariance and
Ledoit-Wolf linear shrinkage in every tested configuration, and is competitive
with POET even in the factor-structured regime where POET is theoretically
optimal. These results motivate NLS as the covariance input to HMVA.

On a fully point-in-time CRSP backtest of the top 100 S\&P~500 stocks from
2002 to 2024, HMVA achieves an annualised Sharpe ratio of 0.807 with a one-year
lookback, versus 0.605 for standard HRP (sample covariance), 0.544 for global
minimum-variance (sample covariance), 0.477 for equal-weight, and 0.503 for the
market-cap benchmark. HMVA also achieves the lowest maximum drawdown ($-34\%$)
and highest Sortino ratio (1.06). The Sharpe advantage over equal-weight is
0.29 and carries a raw block-bootstrap $p$-value of 0.062; it does not achieve
Holm-corrected significance at 5\% but is robust across all 8 cells of the
transaction-cost and lookback robustness sweep.

The stability of HMVA's Sharpe ratio across lookback windows ($T = 252$: 0.807,
$T = 504$: 0.804) is a distinctive feature: the EWMA pre-weighting makes HMVA
largely insensitive to the nominal lookback length, in contrast to HRP and GMV,
whose Sharpe ratios deteriorate with longer lookbacks.

Future work should (i) conduct a full component ablation to isolate the
contribution of each HMVA stage; (ii) extend the experiment to the full
S\&P~500 universe ($N \approx 500$), which would place both lookback
configurations in the high-dimensional and critical regimes where NLS
advantages are stronger; (iii) evaluate HMVA on international equity markets;
and (iv) test whether dynamic (DCC-GARCH) covariance models provide further
improvement over the static rolling NLS estimate.

\chapter{HMVA: Full Algorithm}
\label{app:hmva}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{HMVA at rebalance date $t$}
\label{alg:hmva}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Return window $R \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times N}$, $h=21$, $\delta=2.5$,
         $\tau=0.05$, $\lambda_{\text{scale}}=0.5$, $\lambda_{\text{corr}}=0.2$,
         $\rho_{\text{L2}}=0.1$, $\tau_{\ell_1}=0.05$, previous weights $w_{\text{prev}}$
\Ensure  $w_t \geq 0$, $\mathbf{1}^{\top}w_t = 1$
\State \textbf{Stage 1:} Compute EWMA weights $\{w_s\}_{s=0}^{T-1}$ with $\lambda = 0.5^{1/h}$.
       Form $\tilde{R}_{s,\cdot} = \sqrt{w_s \cdot T}\,R_{s,\cdot}$.
\State \textbf{Stage 2:} $\hat{\Sigma} \leftarrow \mathrm{NLS}(\tilde{R})$.
\State \textbf{Stage 3:} $\pi \leftarrow \delta\hat{\Sigma}\,\mathbf{1}/N$.
       $Q \leftarrow (T^{-1}R^{\top}\mathbf{1}) $ (raw sample means).
       $\mu_{\mathrm{BL}} \leftarrow \mathrm{BlackLitterman}(\hat{\Sigma}, \pi, Q, \tau)$.
\State \textbf{Stage 4:}
       $\mathrm{CV}_{\mathrm{vol}} \leftarrow \mathrm{std}(\sqrt{\mathrm{diag}(\hat{\Sigma})})\,/\,\mathrm{mean}(\sqrt{\mathrm{diag}(\hat{\Sigma})})$.
       $\bar{\rho} \leftarrow \text{mean off-diagonal correlation}(\hat{\Sigma})$.
       $\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}} \leftarrow \mathrm{clip}(\lambda_{\mathrm{scale}}\cdot\mathrm{CV}_{\mathrm{vol}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{corr}}\cdot\bar{\rho},\,0,\,1)$.
       Build tree $\mathcal{T}$ by recursively minimising $f(A,B;\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}})$ in~\eqref{eq:split-obj}.
\State \textbf{Stage 5:} Traverse $\mathcal{T}$ top-down; allocate via~\eqref{eq:sharpe-alloc}; obtain $w$.
\State \textbf{Stage 6a (L2):} $w \leftarrow (1-\rho_{\mathrm{L2}})\,w + \rho_{\mathrm{L2}}/N$.
\State \textbf{Stage 6b (L1):} Apply soft-threshold~\eqref{eq:l1} with $\tau_{\ell_1}$.
\State \Return $w_t \leftarrow w/\mathbf{1}^{\top}w$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\chapter{Statistical Tests: Technical Details}
\label{app:stats}

\section{Diebold-Mariano with Newey-West HAC}

Let $d_t = -r_t^A + r_t^B$ ($A$ is the challenger, $B = $ EW). The
Newey-West HAC estimator of $\mathrm{Var}(\bar{d})$ is
\[
  \hat{V} = \hat{\gamma}_0 + 2\sum_{j=1}^{m}\!\left(1-\frac{j}{m+1}\right)\hat{\gamma}_j,
  \quad
  \hat{\gamma}_j = T^{-1}\sum_{t=j+1}^{T}(d_t - \bar{d})(d_{t-j} - \bar{d}),
\]
with bandwidth $m = \lfloor T^{1/3}\rfloor$. The DM statistic
$\mathrm{DM} = \bar{d}/\sqrt{\hat{V}/T}$ is assessed against $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

\section{Circular block-bootstrap Sharpe test}

Under the null $\mathrm{SR}^A = \mathrm{SR}^{\mathrm{EW}}$, draw $\lceil T/b \rceil$
blocks of length $b = 21$ uniformly with replacement from the circular index set
$\{0,\ldots,T-1\}$ (mod $T$), compute the bootstrapped difference
$(\widehat{\Delta\mathrm{SR}})^*$, and repeat $B = 2000$ times. The empirical
one-sided $p$-value is $B^{-1}\sum_{b=1}^{B}\mathbf{1}[(\widehat{\Delta\mathrm{SR}})^*_b \geq \widehat{\Delta\mathrm{SR}}]$.

\section{Multiple testing corrections}

Given raw $p$-values $p_1 \leq p_2 \leq \cdots \leq p_m$ (sorted):

\textbf{Holm-Bonferroni:} Reject $H_{(i)}$ if $p_{(i)} \leq \alpha/(m-i+1)$,
stopping at the first non-rejection. Strong FWER control at level $\alpha$.

\textbf{Benjamini-Hochberg:} Let $k = \max\{i : p_{(i)} \leq i\alpha/m\}$.
Reject $H_{(1)},\ldots,H_{(k)}$. Controls FDR at level $\alpha$ under independence.
