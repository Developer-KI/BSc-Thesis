\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

This thesis investigates whether conditioning on market regimes, as detected by a
Sparse Jump Model, improves the out-of-sample risk-adjusted performance of a
machine-learning-based portfolio strategy. The strategy combines walk-forward
XGBoost return forecasts for 44 large-cap S\&P~500 stocks with
Ledoit-Wolf-shrunk mean-variance optimisation rebalanced monthly from 2014 to
2026. A two-state Sparse Jump Model is fitted on exponentially-weighted
volatility and downside features of S\&P~500 returns; its online state
predictions are then used to construct regime interaction features that are
appended to the XGBoost feature set. The baseline strategy (without regime
features) achieves an annualised Sharpe ratio of 1.25 over the July~2018 --
November~2024 evaluation window, compared to 1.24 for the naive equal-weight
benchmark and 0.57 for the SPY index. Whether the regime-augmented variant
further improves upon this baseline is the central empirical question. The
paper contributes an end-to-end pipeline that integrates causal regime detection,
cross-sectional characteristic-based forecasting, and robust covariance
estimation, and provides a transparent test of the value of regime information
as a conditioning variable in a large-universe equity portfolio.

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

Two long-standing empirical facts shape this thesis. The first is that
individual stock returns contain predictable cross-sectional variation:
stocks with high momentum, low idiosyncratic volatility, and attractive
valuation ratios have historically earned higher returns than their
counterparts, a pattern documented across decades and geographies
\citep{famafrench1993, jegadeesh1993}. The second is that equity markets move
through regimes: stretches of low volatility and steady appreciation are
punctuated by shorter, sharper episodes of turbulence. These two facts have
mostly been studied in isolation.

This thesis asks whether combining them --- using detected market regimes as
conditioning information inside a machine-learning return-forecasting
pipeline --- delivers better portfolios than either idea alone. The strategy
is built in three layers. A Sparse Jump Model \citep{nystrup2021} detects
market regimes from intraday S\&P~500 volatility and downside features,
producing a daily causal regime label. An XGBoost regressor is trained
monthly on a cross-section of 44 large-cap S\&P~500 stocks to predict
next-month returns, using standard characteristics (momentum, volatility,
valuation, liquidity, quality) augmented with regime interaction features.
The predicted returns then feed a Ledoit-Wolf-shrunk mean-variance optimiser
with a turnover penalty.

The research question is: \emph{Do regime interaction features derived from the
Sparse Jump Model yield a statistically and economically meaningful improvement
in the out-of-sample Sharpe ratio of an XGBoost-driven portfolio strategy?}

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows.
Chapter~\ref{ch:litreview} reviews the relevant literature in four areas:
classical portfolio theory and estimation error, cross-sectional return
predictability and machine learning methods, robust covariance estimation,
and market regime detection. Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} describes the
full pipeline in detail. Chapter~\ref{ch:results} reports out-of-sample
performance. Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} discusses the results and their
limitations, and Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Classical portfolio theory}
\label{sec:lit-classical}

Modern Portfolio Theory originates with \citet{markowitz1952}. Given a vector
of expected returns $\mu \in \mathbb{R}^{N}$ and a covariance matrix
$\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$, the mean-variance optimal portfolio
$w^{\star}$ solves
\begin{equation}
  \min_{w} \; w^{\top} \Sigma w
  \quad \text{s.t.} \quad
  w^{\top} \mu \geq \mu^{\star},
  \quad \mathbf{1}^{\top} w = 1.
  \label{eq:markowitz}
\end{equation}
Varying the target return $\mu^{\star}$ traces the efficient frontier, and
each investor selects the point that matches their risk aversion.
\citet{tobin1958} proved that the presence of a risk-free asset collapses
this choice to a single risky portfolio. \citet{sharpe1964} extended the
framework into equilibrium via the Capital Asset Pricing Model, which
remains the benchmark for measuring systematic risk.

The appeal of mean-variance optimisation rests on strong assumptions ---
stationary return moments, Gaussian returns, and known $\mu$ and $\Sigma$
--- each of which is routinely violated in practice. The third assumption,
that moments are \emph{known} rather than estimated, drives most of the
subsequent literature.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\section{The estimation problem and the 1/N benchmark}
\label{sec:lit-estimation}

In practice $\mu$ and $\Sigma$ are replaced by sample estimates.
\citet{michaud1989} labelled the consequence the ``Markowitz optimisation
enigma'': optimisation amplifies estimation error, because the portfolio
that maximises in-sample utility also maximises sensitivity to in-sample
noise. \citet{chopraziemba1993} quantified the relative damage: errors in
$\hat{\mu}$ are roughly ten times more harmful out of sample than errors in
$\hat{\Sigma}$.

\citet{demiguel2009} provided the most direct empirical challenge. Across
seven datasets and fourteen strategies, no sophisticated allocation method
consistently outperformed the naive $1/N$ equal-weight portfolio once
estimation error was accounted for. Their finding defines a natural baseline:
any strategy must clear the equal-weight hurdle. The present paper uses $1/N$
as its primary benchmark, as well as the S\&P~500 index (SPY) as a passive
market-cap-weighted alternative.

These findings also motivate the use of machine learning for the expected
return input. While the sensitivity of mean-variance to $\hat{\mu}$ is
well documented, the consensus is that the problem lies in using thin
historical averages: \citet{gukellyx2020} show that richly specified
ML models can produce predictive return signals that, when fed into a
portfolio optimiser, yield genuine out-of-sample gains. The key is that
ML forecasts incorporate many predictor signals simultaneously in a
nonlinear way and tend to be better calibrated than moment estimates
from short windows.

\section{Robust covariance estimation}
\label{sec:lit-robust}

The covariance matrix enters mean-variance optimisation through its inverse.
When $N$ is large relative to the estimation window $W$, the sample
covariance $S$ has near-zero eigenvalues that are amplified in $S^{-1}$,
loading the optimiser onto directions of noise rather than signal --- the
Marchenko-Pastur effect. \citet{ledoitwolf2004} address this with a
linear shrinkage estimator
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\Sigma}_{\text{LW}} = \alpha^{\star} F + (1 - \alpha^{\star}) S,
  \label{eq:lw-shrinkage}
\end{equation}
where $F$ is a structured target (constant-correlation covariance), and the
shrinkage intensity $\alpha^{\star} \in [0, 1]$ minimises the expected
Frobenius loss to the true covariance. The closed-form expression for
$\alpha^{\star}$ self-regularises: it increases when $S$ is noisy (short
windows, large universes) and decreases when $S$ is reliable.
\citet{ledoitwolf2003} provide the asymptotic theory;
\citet{ledoitwolf2020} extend the estimator to nonlinear shrinkage that
adjusts each eigenvalue individually, yielding further gains in large
universes.

In the present paper, $\hat{\Sigma}_{\text{LW}}$ is estimated on a
12-month rolling window using the \texttt{scikit-learn} implementation of
\citet{ledoitwolf2004} and is fed directly into the mean-variance
optimiser. Shrinkage ensures that the optimiser receives a well-conditioned
covariance matrix regardless of the number of assets, which is important
because the 44-asset universe is not especially large but the 12-month
window used here is short relative to more conventional two- or three-year
estimates.

\section{Cross-sectional return predictability}
\label{sec:lit-characteristics}

A large empirical literature establishes that individual stock returns are
predictable in the cross-section from observable firm characteristics.
The most influential findings are momentum, value, and size.

\citet{jegadeesh1993} documented that stocks with the highest 3-to-12-month
past returns continue to outperform over the following month (momentum),
a result that has replicated across markets and time periods. The momentum
signal decomposes into multiple horizons: short-term (one month) reversal,
intermediate-term continuation (3--12 months), and long-term (36+ month)
reversal. The 3-, 6-, and 12-month momentum features in this paper capture
the continuation component. \citet{famafrench1993} documented two further
cross-sectional predictors: small firms earn higher returns than large
ones after controlling for beta (size), and high book-to-market firms
earn more than low book-to-market firms (value). Their three-factor
model absorbed much of the Sharpe-CAPM's pricing errors.

The characteristics literature has since expanded considerably.
\citet{amihud2002} showed that illiquidity --- proxied by the average ratio
of absolute daily return to dollar volume --- commands a positive return
premium: investors require compensation for holding stocks that are costly
to trade. The Amihud illiquidity measure enters the feature set here
alongside a volume-trend measure that captures changes in trading activity.
Earnings-to-price, debt-to-equity, return on equity, and revenue growth are
included as quality-and-value signals with documented cross-sectional
predictive power \citep{famafrench2015}.

Idiosyncratic volatility (the residual volatility after removing market
beta) has also attracted attention: \citet{ang2006} found a negative
idiosyncratic-volatility premium in the US, meaning high-idiosyncratic-vol
stocks earn \emph{lower} average returns, a puzzling result that has
generated a large follow-on literature. In this paper it enters as a
feature, allowing the XGBoost model to learn the sign and magnitude of its
predictive content without imposing a prior direction.

This large set of single-predictor findings creates a challenge: combining
predictors linearly in a cross-sectional regression requires strong
functional form assumptions and ignores interactions. Machine learning
methods relax both constraints.

\section{Machine learning in empirical asset pricing}
\label{sec:lit-ml}

\citet{gukellyx2020} provide the most comprehensive evaluation of machine
learning for return prediction in the cross-section of US stocks. Across
neural networks, gradient-boosted trees, random forests, and penalised
regressions, they find that tree-based methods and neural networks materially
outperform OLS and LASSO in terms of out-of-sample $R^{2}$. Crucially, the
gains are not uniform: ML does best when many characteristics are available
and when the true return-predictor relationship is nonlinear. The portfolio
implications are large. Long-short strategies sorted on ML-predicted returns
earn annualised Sharpe ratios several times those of factor-model-sorted
equivalents.

Gradient-boosted trees are particularly competitive in tabular settings.
\citet{chenguest2016} introduced XGBoost, which adds second-order gradient
information and regularisation terms to the basic gradient boosting
framework, making it robust to overfitting on financial data. In a
cross-sectional portfolio application, XGBoost is trained on a panel of
firm-month observations using lagged characteristics as features and the
next month's return as the target. Walk-forward validation --- retrain on a
rolling window, predict on the next month out of sample --- prevents any
form of look-ahead bias \citep{gukellyx2020}.

A key practical issue is that point forecasts from any ML model are
individually noisy. Return shrinkage --- pulling raw predictions toward a
cross-sectional mean prior --- is a standard variance-reduction step in
the literature \citep{james1961}. With a shrink factor $\alpha$ the
shrunken forecast for stock $i$ at month $t$ is
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{\mu}_{i,t} = \alpha \bar{\mu}_{t} + (1 - \alpha) \hat{\mu}_{i,t},
  \label{eq:shrinkage-returns}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\mu}_{t} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i} \hat{\mu}_{i,t}$ is the
cross-sectional average prediction and $\hat{\mu}_{i,t}$ is the raw XGBoost
output. Shrinkage is useful even when the raw predictions are unbiased,
because it lowers the variance of the return vector fed into the
mean-variance optimiser, mitigating the sensitivity documented by
\citet{chopraziemba1993}.

Principal component features extracted from the standardised characteristic
matrix provide an additional source of signal. In each training window,
PCA decomposes the cross-sectional covariance of standardised characteristics
into orthogonal latent factors. Appending the top three PC scores to the
feature set exposes XGBoost to common cross-sectional variation that is not
already captured by individual characteristics, without the curse of
dimensionality that would arise from including all pairwise interactions
explicitly.

\section{Market regime detection: the Sparse Jump Model}
\label{sec:lit-regime}

\subsection{Background and the look-ahead bias problem}

Equity markets alternate between regimes: extended bull periods characterised
by low volatility, positive drift, and mild correlation; and bear episodes
with elevated volatility, negative drift, and rising correlations
\citep{hamilton1989, angbekaert2002}. Exploiting this structure in a
portfolio requires two ingredients: a model that detects regimes, and a
method for incorporating the regime signal without introducing look-ahead
bias.

The look-ahead bias point deserves emphasis. In a hidden-state model, the
smoothed state distribution $\Pr(s_{t} \mid x_{1:T})$ conditions on the
full sample and is therefore unavailable in real time. The filtered
distribution $\Pr(s_{t} \mid x_{1:t})$ is the causal counterpart; it uses
only information available at or before period $t$. Using smoothed states in
a backtest is a severe and hard-to-detect form of data snooping
\citep{nystrup2018}. Every regime inference in this thesis uses the filtered
(or online) distribution exclusively.

\subsection{Sparse Jump Models}

The classical Hidden Markov Model \citep{rabiner1989} estimates the
transition matrix, emission means, and emission covariances via
Expectation-Maximisation. In financial applications its main drawbacks are
that EM can converge to local optima, that the number of free parameters
grows quickly with the state dimension, and that all features are used for
state inference even when only a few carry genuine regime information.

\citet{nystrup2021} proposed the \emph{Sparse Jump Model} (SJM) to address
these limitations. The SJM embeds an $\ell_{1}$ sparsity penalty on feature
weights into the state-inference objective, producing a model that selects
a small, interpretable subset of features as regime discriminators. Formally,
the state sequence is recovered by solving a penalised path problem:
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\{s_t\}} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \ell(x_t, \mu_{s_t}) +
  \lambda \sum_{t=2}^{T} \mathbf{1}[s_t \neq s_{t-1}],
  \label{eq:sjm}
\end{equation}
where $\ell(x_t, \mu_s)$ is a state-conditional loss, $\lambda > 0$ is the
jump penalty that controls regime persistence (larger $\lambda$ means fewer
regime switches), and the $\ell_1$ sparsity acts on the feature-weight
vector inside the loss. The continuous variant replaces hard state
assignments with soft probabilities, yielding smoother transitions and better
calibrated regime probabilities for financial data. Online prediction via
a forward-pass recursion produces causal state assignments at each new
observation, consistent with the no-look-ahead requirement.

The SJM has two properties that are attractive here. First, its sparse
feature selection identifies which aspects of the market's behaviour are
most informative about the current regime, providing an interpretable
regime characterisation. Second, the jump penalty gives the model a natural
persistence prior that reduces high-frequency state-switching driven by
transient noise, avoiding the whipsawing that afflicts simple threshold
rules.

\subsection{Features for regime detection}

Following \citet{nystrup2018}, the input features are computed from daily
log-returns $r_t$ of the S\&P~500 index using exponentially weighted moments
at three half-lives $h \in \{5, 21, 63\}$ trading days:
\begin{align}
  \text{mean}_h(t)      &= \widehat{\text{EWM}}_h[r_t], \\
  \text{logvol}_h(t)    &= \log \widehat{\text{EWM}}_h[\sigma(r)], \\
  \text{downside}_h(t)  &= \sqrt{\widehat{\text{EWM}}_h[r_t^2 \cdot \mathbf{1}_{r_t < 0}]},
  \label{eq:sjm-features}
\end{align}
producing nine features in total. The SJM selects a sparse subset via the
$\ell_1$ penalty; in the present calibration the dominant features are
\texttt{downside\_63} (weight 0.898), \texttt{logvol\_63} (0.711), and
\texttt{downside\_21}, confirming that medium- and long-horizon tail risk
measures are the primary regime discriminators for the S\&P~500 over the
2014--2016 training period.

\section{Synthesis and research gap}
\label{sec:lit-synthesis}

Three strands of literature come together in this paper. The first shows
that cross-sectional stock characteristics predict returns and that ML
methods, especially gradient-boosted trees, aggregate these signals better
than linear alternatives. The second establishes that robust covariance
estimation via Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage substantially reduces the
optimisation error that bedevils mean-variance portfolios. The third
demonstrates that equity markets exhibit persistent regimes, that these
regimes can be detected causally using Sparse Jump Models, and that regime
information alters the cross-sectional properties of return predictors.

What remains unstudied is whether providing regime information
\emph{directly as features} inside the return-forecasting model produces
better portfolios than treating regime and return prediction as
independent steps. The natural way to test this is to run two otherwise
identical pipelines --- XGBoost trained with and without regime interaction
features --- and compare their out-of-sample Sharpe ratios. This is the
experiment this paper conducts.

The closest related work is \citet{wang2020}, who rotate between factor
models depending on the inferred regime, and \citet{millea2023}, who use
reinforcement learning to select among allocation methods as regimes change.
Both papers treat regime conditioning as a \emph{switching} rule applied
after forecasting. The present paper instead treats the regime as an
additional \emph{feature} that the forecasting model can interact with
arbitrary characteristics, which allows XGBoost to learn
regime-dependent characteristic pricing relationships without the author
imposing a parametric form on those interactions.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\section{Data and investment universe}
\label{sec:meth-data}

The investment universe consists of $N = 44$ S\&P~500 large-cap stocks
spanning the period January 2014 to January 2026. The 44 constituents were
selected to represent the largest and most liquid names across major sectors;
the full ticker list is provided in the appendix. Daily adjusted close prices
and trading volumes are downloaded from Yahoo Finance via the
\texttt{yfinance} API. Adjusted prices account for dividends and stock splits
using the split-and-dividend adjustment factor provided by Yahoo Finance.

From daily data, all return and volatility features are computed at the
\emph{monthly} frequency. Month-end prices are taken as the last adjusted
close in each calendar month, and monthly returns are simple percentage
changes:
\begin{equation}
  r_{i,t}^{\text{monthly}} = \frac{P_{i,t} - P_{i,t-1}}{P_{i,t-1}},
\end{equation}
where $P_{i,t}$ is the month-end adjusted close for stock $i$ in month $t$.
Daily return data are used for within-month volatility and tail-risk
estimates that require intra-month granularity.

Portfolio rebalancing is monthly. At each month-end, the model is retrained
on the preceding 36 months, predictions are generated for all assets, and
new target weights are computed. The backtest evaluation period spans
July 2018 to November 2024 (77 monthly periods), using the first 36 months
(2014--2017) for the initial training window and the 12-month lookback for
covariance estimation.

\section{Characteristic-based feature engineering}
\label{sec:meth-features}

For each stock $i$ and month-end $t$, a feature vector $\phi_{i,t}$ is
constructed from the following characteristic groups.

\subsection{Momentum}

Three momentum measures are computed from month-end prices:
\begin{equation}
  \text{mom}_{i,t}^{(k)} = \frac{P_{i,t}}{P_{i,t-k}} - 1,
  \qquad k \in \{3, 6, 12\}.
\end{equation}
These capture short-, intermediate-, and long-horizon price continuation
\citep{jegadeesh1993}.

\subsection{Volatility and tail risk}

Realised volatility is estimated from the preceding 21 trading days of
daily returns and annualised:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{i,t}^{\text{vol}} = \hat{\sigma}_{\text{daily},21} \cdot \sqrt{252}.
\end{equation}
Idiosyncratic volatility is the standard deviation of CAPM residuals over a
30-calendar-day backward-looking window:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{i,t}^{\text{idio}} = \text{std}(r_{i,\tau} - \hat{\beta}_{i,t}\, r_{m,\tau})_{\tau \in [t-30,\, t]}.
\end{equation}
The maximum single-day return within the month ($\text{max\_daily\_ret}$)
captures tail upside, which \citet{bali2011} document as negatively related
to future returns in the cross section.

\subsection{Liquidity}

Two liquidity measures are included. The Amihud illiquidity ratio is
\begin{equation}
  \text{ILLIQ}_{i,t} = \frac{1}{21} \sum_{\tau \in \text{month}} \frac{|r_{i,\tau}|}{V_{i,\tau}^{\$}},
\end{equation}
where $V_{i,\tau}^{\$}$ is dollar trading volume \citep{amihud2002}.
The volume trend is the 12-month percentage change in total monthly volume,
capturing persistent shifts in investor attention.

\subsection{Fundamentals and quality}

Fundamental data are collected from the \texttt{yfinance} \texttt{.info}
API at each rebalance date. Five firm-level signals are used: the trailing
earnings-to-price ratio ($E/P$, the reciprocal of P/E), debt-to-equity
(scaled by $10^{-2}$), return on equity (ROE), revenue growth (as a proxy
for asset growth), and the log of market capitalisation (size). All five
are forward-filled at the monthly frequency. Missing or negative values
are set to \texttt{NaN} and handled by group-wise forward-fill before
the training step.

\subsection{Seasonality}

Calendar-month dummies ($m \in \{1, \ldots, 12\}$) capture
turn-of-the-month, tax-loss selling, and January effects. They are
one-hot encoded with \texttt{get\_dummies} and one category dropped for
identification, yielding 11 binary features.

\subsection{Rolling aggregates and interaction terms}

For each of the four most informative characteristics
(\texttt{mom\_6m}, \texttt{vol\_21d}, \texttt{size}, \texttt{ep\_ratio}),
rolling mean and standard deviation over windows of 3, 6, and 12 months
are appended. These 24 features help the model distinguish whether the
current value of a characteristic is atypically high or low relative to
its own recent history. Two multiplicative interaction terms are also
included: $\text{size} \times \text{mom\_6m}$ and
$\text{vol\_21d} \times \text{mom\_6m}$, capturing the well-documented
interaction between size and momentum and between volatility and momentum.

The full feature vector has approximately 44 dimensions before regime
augmentation (exact count varies slightly because idiosyncratic volatility
is skipped for stocks with insufficient history).

\section{Market regime detection}
\label{sec:meth-sjm}

\subsection{SJM specification}

The market regime $s_t \in \{0, 1\}$ is inferred from daily S\&P~500
log-returns using a Sparse Jump Model with $K = 2$ components, jump penalty
$\lambda = 50$, maximum effective feature count $\kappa^2 = 3.0$, and
continuous state assignments (\texttt{cont=True}). States are sorted
post hoc by cumulative return so that $s_t = 0$ is the bear (low-return)
regime and $s_t = 1$ is the bull (high-return) regime.

\subsection{Feature construction}

The nine-dimensional feature vector $x_t$ is built from daily log-returns
$r_t = \log(P_t / P_{t-1})$ on the S\&P~500 index. For each half-life
$h \in \{5, 21, 63\}$ trading days, three exponentially weighted statistics
are computed:
\begin{align}
  \text{mean}_h(t)      &= \text{EWM}_h[\,r\,]_t, \\
  \text{logvol}_h(t)    &= \log \text{EWM}_h[\,\sigma(r)\,]_t, \\
  \text{downside}_h(t)  &= \sqrt{\text{EWM}_h[\,r^2 \cdot \mathbf{1}_{r < 0}\,]_t},
\end{align}
giving nine features. All features are standardised to zero mean and unit
variance on the training set; the same standardiser is applied to the
online-prediction set.

\subsection{Training and online prediction}

Parameters are estimated on data from January 2014 through December 2015
(the \emph{train window}). After fitting, the \texttt{predict\_online}
method of the \texttt{jumpmodels} library performs a forward-pass recursion
from January 2016 onward, updating the regime probability at each new
day's observation without accessing any future data. This strict causal
evaluation is essential: the forward-pass labels $\hat{s}_t^{\text{online}}$
are the only regime signal used downstream, and they are available at the
end of trading day $t$ without look-ahead.

Feature weights selected by the SJM's $\ell_1$ penalty concentrate on
\texttt{downside\_63} (weight 0.898), \texttt{logvol\_63} (0.711), and
\texttt{downside\_21}, confirming that medium- and long-horizon tail risk
is the primary driver of state transitions in this calibration.

\section{Regime feature augmentation}
\label{sec:meth-regime-features}

The regime-augmented variant appends three types of regime-derived features
to $\phi_{i,t}$ for each stock $i$ and month $t$:

\begin{enumerate}

  \item \textbf{Regime label.} The hard regime assignment
  $\hat{s}_t \in \{0, 1\}$ produced by online SJM prediction,
  resampled from daily to month-end frequency by taking the last
  daily value in the month.

  \item \textbf{Days in regime.} The number of consecutive calendar days
  the S\&P~500 has remained in the current regime as of month-end $t$.
  This captures regime maturity and the tendency for return dynamics to
  evolve within a regime over time.

  \item \textbf{Regime interaction terms.} For each of the 13 ``major''
  characteristics listed in Table~\ref{tab:major-vars}, the elementwise
  product $\hat{s}_t \cdot \phi_{i,t}^{(k)}$ is appended. These terms
  let XGBoost learn a separate relationship between each characteristic
  and future returns for each regime, without the analyst specifying the
  functional form of the conditioning.

\end{enumerate}

This augmentation strategy differs from regime-switching frameworks in the
literature \citep{guidolintimmermann2007, nystrup2018} in that the regime
enters as input to a single forecasting model rather than as a selector
between separate models. XGBoost can therefore discover nonlinear
interaction effects that a parametric regime-switching regression cannot.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\section{Walk-forward XGBoost return forecasting}
\label{sec:meth-xgb}

\subsection{Model specification}

An XGBoost gradient-boosted tree regressor \citep{chenguest2016} is
trained each month on the 36-month rolling cross-sectional panel of
stock--month observations. The response variable is the
next-month return $r_{i,t+1}^{\text{monthly}}$. The XGBoost
hyperparameters are fixed throughout the backtest:

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{XGBoost hyperparameters.}
  \label{tab:xgb-params}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}lr@{}}
    \toprule
    Hyperparameter & Value \\
    \midrule
    \texttt{n\_estimators}    & 150 \\
    \texttt{max\_depth}       & 4 \\
    \texttt{learning\_rate}   & 0.05 \\
    \texttt{subsample}        & 0.70 \\
    \texttt{colsample\_bytree}& 0.50 \\
    \texttt{reg\_lambda}      & 1.0 \\
    \texttt{reg\_alpha}       & 0.1 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Subsampling of both observations and features at each tree step, together
with the $L_2$ (\texttt{reg\_lambda}) and $L_1$ (\texttt{reg\_alpha})
penalties on leaf weights, provide regularisation against overfitting on the
36-month training panel.

\subsection{PCA augmentation}

Before training, the feature matrix $X_{\text{train}} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$
is standardised to zero mean and unit variance. Three principal components
are then extracted from the standardised matrix and appended as additional
columns, so the effective training matrix is
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{X}_{\text{train}} = \bigl[X_{\text{train}} \;\big|\; U_3 \bigr],
\end{equation}
where $U_3 \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times 3}$ contains the first three PC scores.
The same scaler and PCA object (fitted on the training window) are applied
to the test-month observations before prediction, ensuring no information
leakage. PCA augmentation exposes the model to common cross-sectional
variation in characteristics that individual feature interactions may not
capture directly.

\subsection{Walk-forward validation}

For each month $t$ from the first prediction month onward:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Collect all stock--month observations from months $t - W$ to $t - 1$
        (training set, $W = 36$).
  \item Fit the standardiser, PCA, and XGBoost model on this set.
  \item Construct features for all stocks at month $t$ using the fitted
        standardiser and PCA.
  \item Generate raw predictions $\hat{\mu}_{i,t}$ for all stocks
        at month $t$.
\end{enumerate}
No information from month $t$ onward enters the model trained for month $t$.
This strict sequential structure ensures that performance metrics computed
on the evaluation period are genuinely out of sample.

\subsection{Return shrinkage}

Raw XGBoost predictions are shrunken toward the cross-sectional mean:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{\mu}_{i,t} = \alpha\,\bar{\mu}_{t} + (1 - \alpha)\,\hat{\mu}_{i,t},
  \qquad \bar{\mu}_{t} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N} \hat{\mu}_{j,t},
  \label{eq:meth-shrinkage}
\end{equation}
with shrink factor $\alpha = 0.25$. Shrinkage reduces the variance of the
return-vector input to the optimiser and partially corrects for the tendency
of boosted trees to produce extreme predictions for individual stocks when the
training window contains unusual observations.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\section{Covariance estimation and portfolio optimisation}
\label{sec:meth-opt}

\subsection{Ledoit-Wolf covariance}

At each month-end $t$, the monthly return matrix over the preceding
$W_{\Sigma} = 12$ months is collected for all assets with complete data.
The Ledoit-Wolf linear shrinkage estimator of \citet{ledoitwolf2004}
is applied:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\Sigma}_{t} = \alpha^{\star}_{t}\, F_{t} + (1 - \alpha^{\star}_{t})\, S_{t},
  \label{eq:meth-lw}
\end{equation}
where $F_t$ is the constant-correlation target and $\alpha^{\star}_t$ is
the analytically optimal shrinkage intensity. The 12-month window was
chosen to balance the need for a recent estimate against the need for
enough observations to produce a non-degenerate $\hat{\Sigma}_t$; with
12 monthly observations and 44 assets, the matrix is rank-deficient without
shrinkage, which makes the \citet{ledoitwolf2004} estimator strictly necessary
for this configuration.

\subsection{Mean-variance optimisation with turnover penalty}

Given the shrunken expected return vector $\tilde{\mu}_{t}$ and the
Ledoit-Wolf covariance $\hat{\Sigma}_{t}$, the portfolio weight vector
$w_t$ is the solution to
\begin{equation}
  \max_{w} \;\tilde{\mu}_{t}^{\top} w - \frac{\lambda}{2}\, w^{\top} \hat{\Sigma}_{t}\, w
  - \gamma\, \|w - w_{t-1}^{\text{drift}}\|_{1}
  \quad \text{s.t.} \quad
  \mathbf{1}^{\top} w = 1, \quad w \geq 0,
  \label{eq:meth-mv}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda = 2.5$ is the risk-aversion parameter, $\gamma = 0.01$ is
the turnover penalty, and $w_{t-1}^{\text{drift}}$ is the vector of weights
as they drift from the previous month's target after one period of returns
(not the same as $w_{t-1}$ when assets have returned different amounts).
Problem~\eqref{eq:meth-mv} is solved with SLSQP via \texttt{scipy.optimize.minimize}.

The turnover penalty introduces an incentive to stay close to the current
portfolio, reducing transaction costs and preventing the optimiser from making
large, potentially noise-driven reallocations in response to small changes
in the predicted returns. Unlike an explicit transaction cost, the $L_1$
penalty on weight changes is computationally tractable and produces sparse
differences between consecutive portfolios.

The risk-aversion parameter $\lambda = 2.5$ sits in the range typically
associated with institutional investors and produces well-diversified
portfolios in calibration tests; results under $\lambda \in \{1, 5\}$ are
reported as robustness checks in the supplementary material.

\section{Benchmarks and evaluation criteria}
\label{sec:meth-benchmarks}

\subsection{Benchmark strategies}

Four strategies are compared over the evaluation period:

\begin{enumerate}

  \item \textbf{XGBoost + Markowitz (baseline).} The full pipeline described
  in Sections~\ref{sec:meth-features}--\ref{sec:meth-opt} without regime
  features. This is the primary strategy and the baseline against which
  regime augmentation is tested.

  \item \textbf{XGBoost + Markowitz (regime-augmented).} The same pipeline
  with the regime label, days-in-regime, and 13 interaction terms appended
  to the feature set. This is the treatment strategy. Its Sharpe ratio
  difference from the baseline is the main test statistic.

  \item \textbf{$\mathbf{1/N}$ equal-weight} \citep{demiguel2009}.
  $w_{i,t} = 1/N$ for all $i$ and $t$. No estimated parameters. The most
  parsimonious baseline and the standard hurdle in the portfolio literature.

  \item \textbf{S\&P~500 (SPY).} The passive market-cap-weighted index
  return. Represents the opportunity cost of active management and controls
  for broad market beta.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Evaluation metrics}

Performance is evaluated along four dimensions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Annualised return}: $\hat{R}^{p} = 12 \times \bar{r}^{p}$,
  where $\bar{r}^{p}$ is the mean monthly portfolio return.

  \item \textbf{Annualised volatility}: $\hat{\sigma}^{p} = \sqrt{12} \times s^{p}$,
  where $s^{p}$ is the sample standard deviation of monthly returns.

  \item \textbf{Sharpe ratio}:
  $\text{SR} = (\bar{r}^{p} - r_f/12) / s^{p} \times \sqrt{12}$,
  using an annualised risk-free rate $r_f = 4\%$.

  \item \textbf{Maximum drawdown}: the deepest trough-to-peak decline in
  cumulative wealth over the evaluation period.
\end{itemize}

Differences in Sharpe ratios between the baseline and regime-augmented
strategies are tested for statistical significance using the robust Sharpe
test of \citet{ledoitwolf2008}, which accounts for non-Gaussian returns and
serial correlation. Because two nested variants are compared (one pipeline
with and without a feature block), the test statistic has a natural
interpretation: a positive and statistically significant Sharpe increment
supports the value of regime information as a conditioning variable.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

\section{Interpretation of the baseline result}

The baseline XGBoost + Markowitz strategy achieves a Sharpe ratio of 1.25
over the evaluation period, clearing the equal-weight benchmark by a slim
margin (1.24) while generating substantially higher absolute returns (57.3\%
versus 26.8\%). This pattern --- higher return at higher volatility, similar
Sharpe --- is consistent with the portfolio optimiser concentrating in
high-momentum tech-sector names that dominated S\&P~500 performance over the
2018--2024 period. The regime-conditional analysis will determine whether
this concentration is rational given the prevailing market state or whether
it represents an under-diversification that regime conditioning can correct.

\section{Limitations}

Several limitations should be noted. First, the 44-stock universe is a
convenience sample biased toward large, liquid names. Survivorship bias
may be present if all 44 tickers were current constituents at the time of
data download. Second, fundamental data (E/P, D/E, ROE) are collected at
the time of analysis rather than as point-in-time historical snapshots; this
introduces a mild look-ahead bias in the fundamental features. Third, the
Ledoit-Wolf covariance is estimated on a 12-month window that is shorter
than the 36-month feature window, creating a potential inconsistency in the
information sets used for return and risk estimation. Fourth, transaction
costs are captured only through the turnover penalty $\gamma$ in the
optimiser rather than as explicit bid-ask spreads; results may overstate
net-of-cost performance. These limitations define priorities for future work.

\chapter{Conclusion}
\label{ch:conclusion}

This paper develops and evaluates an end-to-end pipeline for large-cap US
equity portfolio construction that combines three ideas: walk-forward XGBoost
return forecasting on a rich cross-section of stock characteristics,
Ledoit-Wolf-shrunk mean-variance optimisation with a turnover penalty, and
Sparse Jump Model regime detection used to generate regime interaction
features. The baseline strategy achieves an annualised Sharpe ratio of 1.25
over July 2018 to November 2024, clearing the 1/N equal-weight benchmark
(1.24) and substantially outperforming passive SPY (0.57).

The central contribution is a transparent test of whether SJM regime
features improve cross-sectional return forecasting inside a gradient-boosted
tree model. If the regime-augmented variant materially improves on the
baseline Sharpe ratio, the finding supports the view that market regimes
shift the pricing of cross-sectional characteristics and that this shift can
be detected causally in real time using sparse jump models. A negative
result would be equally informative: it would suggest that the marginal
predictive content of regime information is already captured by the
momentum and volatility characteristics in the baseline feature set.

Future work could extend the universe to the full S\&P~500, use point-in-time
fundamental data to eliminate the mild look-ahead in firm characteristics,
and explore more expressive regime models with more than two states or
soft-probability weighting of the interaction features.
