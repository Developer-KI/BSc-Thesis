3 and THE PROBLEM (topic intro)
The topic

Optimal portfolios for a large universe of assets

Markowitz mean variance optimisation is the textbook answer is hold the portfolio that best trades off risk against return. In practice it rarely survives contact with real data.

The inputs it needs
w∝Σ−1 μ
A return signal μ and a covariance matrix Σ and both estimated, both noisy, then inverted.
i.
The return signal barely predicts. Out of sample R² of the best predictors sits near 1% and the signal is almost all noise.
ii.
The covariance is hard to estimate. With many assets relative to the sample, there are too many entries to pin down precisely.
↯
Inversion amplifies both. Matrix inversion loads hardest on the smallest, least reliable eigenvalues and turning estimation error into unstable, concentrated bets.
The question. Can we target risk adjusted return out of sample without ever inverting an estimated covariance matrix?

4 and WHY MVO FAILS (literature)
Literature · the diagnosis

“Optimization error maximization”

Six decades of work converge on one verdict is the optimizer doesn't cancel estimation error, it magnifies it.

Michaud
(1989)
Names the pathology is the optimizer behaves like an error maximizer.
Chopra Ziemba
(1993)
Errors in means hurt approximately 10 times more than errors in covariances.
Weight sensitivity
A tiny change in expected return estimates can produce a large change in mean variance weights. This is the intuition behind optimization error maximization.
1/N
DeMiguel et al. (2009). Across 14 strategies and 7 datasets, no sophisticated optimizer reliably beat the naïve equal weight portfolio. This is the hurdle every new method must clear.

5 and LITERATURE LANDSCAPE → GAP
Literature · the responses & the gap

Two repair traditions and and the opening between them

Fix the inputs
·
Shrink the covariance. Ledoit & Wolf (2004, 2020) and linear, then nonlinear eigenvalue shrinkage
·
Stabilise the returns. Black Litterman (1992) blends a prior with views, momentum and factors (Jegadeesh Titman 1993, Fama French)
·
Constrain the weights. Jagannathan & Ma (2003) and no short sale equivalency with implicit shrinkage
Re-design the construction
·
Hierarchical Risk Parity. López de Prado (2016) and cluster the correlation matrix, allocate risk through a tree, never invert Σ.
The gap. HRP solved noise robustness for risk allocation and but no construction has done the same for mean-variance (known as risk adjusted returns) out of sample. That is this thesis's contribution.

7 and HMVA OVERVIEW
HMVA · the idea

One decision, four stages and no matrix ever inverted

Stage 1

Weight & estimate

Use the return window, transform it using exponential weighting, then estimate μ and Σ.

→
Stage 2

Build the tree

Split the universe top down so the two branches co move as little as possible.

→
Stage 3

Allocate recursively

Send capital down the tree in proportion to each branch's Sharpe proxy.

→
Stage 4

Filter turnover

Blend new weights with old via a Kalman type adaptive gain filter.

Fallback mechanism
No positive branch signal means inverse variance bisection
When both child clusters have non positive Sharpe proxies, HMVA stops trusting the return signal at that split and allocates by inverse variance instead. This keeps the portfolio long only and prevents noisy expected returns from forcing concentrated bets.
Methods · 2.1
8 and TREE CONSTRUCTION
Stage 2 · the hierarchy

Splits that minimise how much the branches co move

Split objective and minimised over bipartitions (L, R)
f(L,R)=CovEW(L,R)+CorrEW(L,R)
Both terms are computed from the equal weighted sub portfolios of each branch and not the nearest asset pair. Small covariance ⇒ little absolute co movement and small correlation ⇒ little relative co movement.

Evaluating whole sub portfolios and rather than single linkages as in standard HRP and means diversification is measured at exactly the level where capital is allocated. Branches end up with predictable total risk.

Methods · 2.1
9 and RECURSIVE ALLOCATION
Stage 3 · recursive allocation

Capital follows risk adjusted return down the tree

At each node and split by cluster Sharpe proxy
αL=S(L)S(L) + S(R)
S(C) = equal weight return ÷ equal weight volatility of the cluster, floored at zero. The branch with the higher Sharpe proxy receives more capital.

Because allocation is recursive, the tree structure itself bounds the maximum weight on any one cluster and implicit concentration limits, no hard caps.

Fallback and no positive signal
αL∝IV(L)=1√ VarEW(L)
The signal only ever tilts between branches and it is never inverted onto individual weights, so a noisy μ cannot concentrate the book.
Methods · 2.1
10 and KALMAN FILTER
Stage 4 · turnover control

A Kalman type gain that moves only when it should

Smoothed weights and signal to noise convex blend
w̃=K wnew+(1−K) wold|K=YX + Y
X and spectral entropy of Σ is how diffuse the risk structure is. Y and relative change in μ is how much the signal just moved.
Diffuse covariance, stable signal
K → 0 · the portfolio barely moves.
Sharp signal shift
K → 1 · it updates aggressively toward the new target.

Turnover and and trading cost and is spent only when new information justifies it.

11 and ESTIMATION
Estimation · the inputs

Best in class estimators and so the comparison is fair

Covariance · Nonlinear shrinkage
Ledoit & Wolf (2020)
ΣNLS=U diag(λi⋆) U⊤
Keep the eigenvectors and shrink each eigenvalue individually toward its oracle value. Undoes the eigenvalue dispersion that destabilises any optimizer.
Return · Posterior return signal
μPRS=π+Σ(Σ+diagΣ)−1(Q−π)
A cross sectional prior π blended with a skip a month momentum view Q. More stable than the raw sample mean.
Every strategy gets these same inputs. So any difference in performance is attributable to the construction mechanism and not to the data each method is allowed to see.

12 and IMPLEMENTATION
Implementation · making it scale

Exact where it's cheap, near exact where it isn't

The only step that can't scale is the split is a cluster of size n has 2n−1−1 possible bipartitions and fine near the leaves, intractable near the root.

Small
n ≤ 10
Exhaustive search. Enumerate every bipartition, return the global optimum of f.
Large
n > 10
Contiguous cut heuristic. Sort by covariance row sum, prefix sum once in O(n²), pick the best of n−1 cuts in a single pass.
~50%
of clusters is heuristic recovers the exact partition
O(n²)
one prefix sum, then constant time cut scoring

On the rest it stays close in objective value and validated against the exhaustive optimum in the appendix. Full pseudo code accompanies one rebalance date.

20 and CONCLUSION
Conclusion

A noise robust route to mean variance, out of sample

What it delivers
✓
Highest full sample Sharpe of seven strategies clearing the 1/N hurdle and beating MVO from identical inputs by ≈60%.
✓
Better return per unit of risk with improved drawdown behavior rather than performance bought with tail risk.
✓
Alpha localised to crises, robust across costs and lookbacks.
Limits and what comes next
✓
One market and asset class large cap US means the harder test is the full S&P 500 at N/T ≈ 1.
✓
Statistical power remains limited the two decade horizon supports direction and result suggestion, not formal significance.
✓
Next steps clarify the mechanism study the tree, bisection, and filter components, then replace filter heuristics with a latent state model.
